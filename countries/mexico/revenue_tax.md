# Mexico - Business Revenue Tax Codes

> **Jurisdiction**: Servicio de Administración Tributaria (SAT)
> **Governing Law**: Ley del Impuesto Sobre la Renta (LISR), Ley del IVA, Código Fiscal de la Federación
> **Tax Year**: 2024/2025
> **Currency**: Mexican Peso (MXN / $)

## 10. Annual Inflation Adjustment (Ajuste Anual por Inflación)

| Parameter | Value |
|---|---|
| Concept | Monetary position gain/loss due to inflation on debts vs. credits |
| If debts > credits (net debtor) | Inflation gain is **taxable income** |
| If credits > debts (net creditor) | Inflation loss is **deductible** |
| Calculation | Based on INPC (National Consumer Price Index) monthly averages |
| Filing | Computed annually and included in annual return |

## 13. Income by Source Type

> **Legal basis**: LISR (various articles); Ley del IVA; CFF

### 13.1 Service Income (Domestic & Cross-Border)

| Parameter | Value |
|---|---|
| Domestic services | Taxed as ordinary business income at **30% ISR** — LISR Art. 9 |
| IVA on services | **16%** standard rate — Ley del IVA Art. 1 |
| Cross-border services to Mexico (non-residents) | ISR WHT **25%** on gross or optional **35%** on net — LISR Art. 153-156 |
| Professional/technical services by non-residents | ISR WHT rates under LISR Title V (Arts. 153-175) |
| Transfer pricing | OECD-aligned; master file + local file + CbCR required — LISR Art. 76, 76-A |
| Digital platform services | ISR retention **1-4%** depending on service type — LISR Art. 113-A, 113-B |
| Maquila regime | Safe harbor transfer pricing rules — LISR Art. 181-182 |

**Practical notes:**
- Domestic service income is included in the general taxable base; no separate rate applies beyond the standard 30% corporate ISR.
- For cross-border services, the non-resident provider may elect to be taxed on net income (35%) by appointing a legal representative in Mexico, instead of the default 25% gross withholding.
- Digital platform retention rates vary: transportation services (2.1%), accommodation (4%), sale of goods (0.4-5.4%), content streaming/download (1%).
- Maquiladoras operating under LISR Art. 181-182 can satisfy transfer pricing obligations through safe harbor computations (6.9% of total assets or 6.5% of operating costs, whichever is greater).

### 13.2 Royalty & IP Licensing Income (Received)

| Parameter | Value |
|---|---|
| Domestic royalties received | Included in taxable income at **30% ISR** |
| Royalty payments to non-residents (general) | ISR WHT — LISR Art. 167 |
| — Patents, trade names, trademarks | **25%** |
| — Copyright (literary/artistic) | **25%** |
| — Technical assistance | **25%** |
| — Treaty rates (typical) | **10-12%** |
| IP amortization | **15%** annual straight-line for patents/trademarks — LISR Art. 33(IV) |
| Patent box regime | **Not available** in Mexico |
| CUFIN implications | Royalty income contributes to CUFIN (net after-tax profit account) |

**Practical notes:**
- Mexico does not have a patent box or IP box regime. All IP income is taxed at the general 30% corporate rate.
- Royalties paid to non-residents are subject to 25% WHT under domestic law; however, Mexico's extensive treaty network (60+ treaties) typically reduces this to 10-12%.
- IP amortization at 15% per year applies to intangible assets with a defined useful life (patents, trademarks, franchises). Goodwill is not amortizable.
- Royalty income earned by a Mexican resident company is included in its CUFIN balance after ISR has been paid, which affects future dividend distributions.

### 13.3 Rental & Lease Income

| Parameter | Value |
|---|---|
| Real property rental (corporate) | Taxed at **30% ISR** — LISR Art. 9 |
| Real property rental (individuals) | Option for **35% blind deduction** on gross rental income — LISR Art. 115 |
| Equipment leasing | Ordinary income at **30% ISR** |
| Financial leasing | Lessor reports interest component; lessee depreciates the asset — LISR Art. 38 |
| IVA on rental income | **16%** — commercial property |
| Residential housing rental IVA | **Exempt** — LIVA Art. 20(II) |
| Real property rental by non-residents | ISR WHT **25%** of gross — LISR Art. 158 |
| FIBRA (REIT equivalent) | Tax-transparent vehicle; **30% ISR WHT** on distributions to investors |

**Practical notes:**
- Individuals earning rental income may elect the 35% blind deduction (deducción ciega) under LISR Art. 115, which allows them to deduct 35% of gross rental income in lieu of actual expenses plus an additional deduction for property tax paid.
- Financial leases are bifurcated: the lessor recognizes interest income (accrual basis), while the lessee treats the asset as owned for depreciation purposes under LISR Art. 38.
- FIBRAs (Fideicomisos de Infraestructura y Bienes Raíces) are tax-transparent; the trust itself does not pay ISR, but distributions to certificate holders are subject to 30% ISR withholding (reduced by treaty for non-residents).
- Residential housing rental is exempt from IVA under LIVA Art. 20(II), but this means the landlord cannot credit input IVA on related expenses.

### 13.4 Capital Gains (Business Assets, Shares, Property)

| Parameter | Value |
|---|---|
| Corporate capital gains | Included in taxable income at **30% ISR** |
| Sale of shares by residents | Gain determined using average cost formula adjusted for inflation — LISR Art. 22-23 |
| Sale of shares by non-residents | **25%** of gross OR **35%** of net gain — LISR Art. 161 |
| Stock exchange listed shares (residents) | **10% ISR** on gains — LISR Art. 129 |
| Real property capital gain | **30% ISR**; individuals can elect **35%** on net gain |
| Inflation adjustment (INPC) | Cost basis adjusted for inflation — LISR Art. 124 |
| CUFIN | Reinvested profit account reduces taxable gain on share disposal — LISR Art. 77-78 |
| Merger/spin-off | Tax-neutral if meeting requirements — CFF Art. 14-B, LISR Art. 24 |

**Practical notes:**
- The average cost per share method under LISR Art. 22-23 accounts for accumulated earnings (CUFIN), paid-in capital (CUCA), and inflation adjustments. This can significantly reduce the taxable gain.
- Non-residents selling shares may elect to pay 35% on net gain (instead of 25% on gross) by appointing a legal representative who files with SAT and provides audited financial statements.
- The 10% rate on listed share gains (LISR Art. 129) applies to individuals and is withheld by the brokerage firm; this is a final tax.
- For mergers and spin-offs to qualify as tax-neutral under CFF Art. 14-B, the transaction must meet substance requirements including continuation of business activities and maintenance of ownership for at least 2 years.
- INPC inflation adjustment on cost basis can be substantial in Mexico given historical inflation rates; the adjustment is calculated from the month of acquisition to the month of disposal.

### 13.5 Dividend Income (Received by Company)

| Parameter | Value |
|---|---|
| Corporate dividends from CUFIN | No additional ISR (already taxed at corporate level) |
| Dividends exceeding CUFIN | Additional **30% ISR** payable by distributing company (piggybacked) — LISR Art. 10 |
| Dividends to individuals | Additional **10% ISR** on post-2013 profits — LISR Art. 140 |
| Dividends to non-residents | **10% ISR WHT** — LISR Art. 164(I) |
| Intercorporate dividends | Tax-free if from CUFIN within corporate group |
| Foreign dividends received | Included in worldwide income at **30% ISR**; FTC available |

**Practical notes:**
- CUFIN (Cuenta de Utilidad Fiscal Neta) is the cumulative after-tax profit account maintained by every Mexican entity. Dividends paid from CUFIN have already borne corporate ISR and are not subject to additional corporate-level tax.
- When dividends exceed the CUFIN balance, the distributing company must pay an additional 30% ISR on the excess amount, grossed up by a factor of 1.4286 (the "piggybacking" mechanism under LISR Art. 10). This ISR can be credited in subsequent years.
- The 10% ISR on dividends to individuals (LISR Art. 140) applies only to profits generated from January 1, 2014 onward. Pre-2014 CUFIN balances are exempt from this additional 10%.
- Foreign dividends received by a Mexican resident company are included in taxable income and subject to 30% ISR, but a foreign tax credit is available for taxes paid abroad, limited on a per-country basis.

### 13.6 Interest Income (Received)

| Parameter | Value |
|---|---|
| Corporate interest income | Included in taxable income at **30% ISR** |
| Inflation adjustment | Monetary position gain/loss system — LISR Art. 44-46 |
| — Taxable component | Only real interest (after inflation adjustment) is taxable |
| Interest on government bonds (CETES) | ISR withheld at source (provisional rate) |
| Bank deposit interest | ISR provisional withholding at annual rate set by Ley de Ingresos |
| Interest to non-residents | ISR WHT **4.9-35%** depending on type and treaty — LISR Art. 166 |
| — Bank-to-bank | **4.9%** — LISR Art. 166(I)(a) |
| — Government bonds placed abroad | **10%** |
| — Other | **15-35%** |
| Related-party interest | Arm's length + **30% EBITDA** cap — LISR Art. 28(XXXII) |

**Practical notes:**
- Mexico's inflation adjustment system (ajuste anual por inflación) under LISR Art. 44-46 means that only the real component of interest income is effectively taxed. The inflationary component is neutralized through the annual inflation adjustment calculation.
- The provisional ISR withholding rate on bank deposit interest is set each year by the Ley de Ingresos de la Federación. For 2024, the rate is 0.15% annualized on the principal balance.
- For non-resident interest payments, the preferential 4.9% rate (LISR Art. 166(I)(a)) applies to interest paid to foreign banks registered with SHCP, provided the loan is at arm's length.
- The 30% EBITDA cap on interest deductions (LISR Art. 28(XXXII)) applies to net interest expense exceeding MXN 20 million annually. Excess non-deductible interest can be carried forward for 10 years.

## 14. Sector-Specific Revenue Taxes

> **Legal basis**: LISR, LIVA, Ley del IEPS, Ley Federal de Derechos, and sector-specific legislation

### 14.1 Digital Services Taxation

| Parameter | Value |
|---|---|
| Standalone DST law | **No** — Mexico has no separate Digital Services Tax |
| Digital platform ISR regime | Platforms withhold **1-4%** ISR on gross income — LISR Art. 113-A, 113-B |
| — Transportation | **2.1%** |
| — Accommodation | **4%** |
| — Sale of goods | **0.4-5.4%** (depending on monthly income) |
| — Content streaming/download | **1%** |
| IVA on digital services by non-residents | **16%** — LIVA Art. 18-B, 18-D through 18-J |
| Non-resident digital provider IVA registration | **Required** |
| CFDI requirements | Mandatory for all digital transactions |

**Practical notes:**
- Mexico does not levy a standalone Digital Services Tax (DST) like some European countries. Instead, digital services are taxed through the existing ISR and IVA frameworks with specific provisions for digital platforms.
- Non-resident digital service providers (e.g., Netflix, Spotify, Uber, Amazon) must register for IVA purposes in Mexico and collect and remit 16% IVA on services provided to Mexican consumers (LIVA Art. 18-B through 18-J, effective June 1, 2020).
- The ISR retention rates under LISR Art. 113-A vary by activity type and income level. Individuals operating through digital platforms may opt to treat these withholdings as final payments if their annual income does not exceed MXN 300,000.
- CFDI (Comprobante Fiscal Digital por Internet) electronic invoicing is required for all digital transactions to ensure tax compliance and traceability.

### 14.2 Natural Resource / Extractive Taxes

| Parameter | Value |
|---|---|
| **Mining royalties** | |
| Special mining right | **7.5%** on EBITDA — Ley Federal de Derechos Art. 268-270 |
| Extraordinary mining right | **0.5%** on gross revenue from gold/silver |
| Additional mining right | Surcharge on unused concessions |
| **Oil & gas** | |
| Regime | Pemex profit-sharing + entitlements regime — Ley de Ingresos sobre Hidrocarburos |
| Shared Utility Contracts (CUC) | Progressive sliding scale |
| License contracts | Royalty (**7.5%**) + corporate ISR (**30%**) + additional consideration |
| State Participation Fund | Hydrocarbons revenue distributed to states |
| **IEPS on fuels** | Per-liter specific rates on gasoline, diesel — Ley del IEPS Art. 2-A |

**Practical notes:**
- The mining tax regime was introduced in 2014 with three components: the special mining right (7.5% on EBITDA), the extraordinary mining right (0.5% on precious metals gross revenue), and the additional mining right (surcharge on concessions not exploited for two consecutive years).
- Oil and gas taxation follows the 2014 energy reform framework. Shared Utility Contracts (CUC) use a progressive sliding scale where the government's share increases as cumulative production or prices rise. License contracts combine a 7.5% royalty with standard 30% ISR.
- IEPS on fuels (gasoline and diesel) is calculated on a per-liter basis with rates adjusted weekly by SHCP. The mechanism includes a carbon tax component and a fossil fuel component.
- The State Participation Fund (Fondo Mexicano del Petróleo) distributes hydrocarbon revenues to states and municipalities per constitutional mandate.

### 14.3 Insurance Premium Taxes

| Parameter | Value |
|---|---|
| Specific insurance premium tax (IPT) | **Not applicable** — Mexico has no dedicated IPT |
| Insurance companies ISR | Standard **30% ISR** |
| ISR WHT on reinsurance premiums to non-residents | **2-25%** depending on treaty — LISR Art. 163 |
| IVA on insurance | **Exempt** — LIVA Art. 15(IX); except certain casualty insurance |
| CNSF regulatory fees | National Insurance and Bonding Commission fees apply |
| Mathematical reserves | **Deductible** for ISR purposes — LISR Art. 53 |

**Practical notes:**
- Unlike many jurisdictions, Mexico does not impose a separate Insurance Premium Tax (IPT). Insurance operations are subject to the general corporate income tax regime at 30%.
- Insurance services are generally exempt from IVA under LIVA Art. 15(IX), which means insurers cannot credit input IVA on their operational expenses. Certain casualty insurance policies may be subject to 16% IVA.
- Reinsurance premiums paid to non-resident reinsurers are subject to ISR withholding rates between 2% and 25%, with most tax treaties reducing the rate significantly.
- Mathematical reserves (technical provisions) are deductible for ISR purposes under LISR Art. 53, subject to actuarial certification and CNSF (Comisión Nacional de Seguros y Fianzas) regulatory requirements.

### 14.4 Financial Transaction Taxes

| Parameter | Value |
|---|---|
| IDE (Tax on Cash Deposits) | **REPEALED** in 2014 (was 3% on monthly cash deposits >$15,000 MXN) |
| Current FTT | **None** |
| ISR on interest from financial system | Provisional withholding rates set annually |
| Stock exchange capital gains | ISR **10%** on gains from listed shares |
| Real estate transfer tax (ISABI) | State/municipal level, typically **2-5%** of property value |
| Stamp duty | **Not applicable** in Mexico |

**Practical notes:**
- The IDE (Impuesto a los Depósitos en Efectivo) was a 3% tax on monthly cash deposits exceeding MXN 15,000, designed as an anti-informality measure. It was repealed effective January 1, 2014 as part of the fiscal reform.
- Mexico does not currently impose any Financial Transaction Tax (FTT). Legislative proposals have surfaced periodically but none have been enacted.
- The 10% ISR on stock exchange capital gains (LISR Art. 129) applies to individuals on gains from the sale of listed shares. The brokerage firm withholds and remits.
- ISABI (Impuesto Sobre Adquisición de Inmuebles) is a state/municipal-level transfer tax on real property acquisitions, typically ranging from 2% to 5% of the higher of the transaction value or cadastral value, depending on the state.
- Mexico does not have a stamp duty (timbres fiscales) on legal documents or transactions, unlike some Latin American jurisdictions.

### 14.5 Gaming / Betting Revenue Taxes

| Parameter | Value |
|---|---|
| IEPS on gaming/betting | **30%** on net gaming revenue — Ley del IEPS Art. 2(II)(B) |
| ISR on prizes | **1%** federal ISR + state tax (typically **6%**) — LISR Art. 137-138 |
| IVA on gaming | **16%** |
| State gaming taxes | Additional **2-10%** depending on state |
| Lottery prizes | **1% ISR** federal — LISR Art. 137; + state tax (varies) |
| Online gaming | Subject to same IEPS + ISR framework |
| SEGOB licensing | Interior Ministry (Secretaría de Gobernación) permits required |

**Practical notes:**
- Gaming and betting operations in Mexico are subject to a layered tax structure: IEPS at 30% on net gaming revenue, IVA at 16% on gaming services, and ISR on prizes distributed to winners.
- The 1% federal ISR on prizes (LISR Art. 137) is withheld by the gaming operator at the time of prize payment. State-level taxes on prizes vary significantly, with most states imposing 6% but some as low as 2% or as high as 10%.
- Online gaming and betting platforms are subject to the same IEPS and ISR framework as physical establishments. Digital platform operators must register and comply with CFDI issuance requirements.
- SEGOB (Secretaría de Gobernación) is the federal authority that grants gaming permits and licenses. Operating without a valid SEGOB permit is a criminal offense under the Ley Federal de Juegos y Sorteos.
- All gaming establishments must maintain electronic monitoring systems (CJS — Centro de Justicia y Seguridad) for regulatory compliance and tax audit purposes.

### 14.6 Agricultural Income Taxation

| Parameter | Value |
|---|---|
| AGAPE regime | Preferential treatment for agriculture, livestock, fishing, forestry — LISR Art. 74-75 |
| Individual exemption | Up to **40 UMA** annual (≈MXN 423,200 in 2024) |
| Corporate exemption | Up to **20 UMA** annual per partner (max **200 UMA** total) |
| Income above exemption | **30% ISR** (individuals can use reduced rates) |
| IVA on agricultural inputs | **0%** — LIVA Art. 2-A |
| Ejido (communal land) income | Special provisions under LISR |
| IEPS on pesticides | **6-9%** based on toxicity — Ley del IEPS Art. 2(I)(I) |
| PTU (profit sharing) | Applies to agricultural companies with workers |
| Simplified accounting | Available for qualifying agricultural businesses |

**Practical notes:**
- The AGAPE (Actividades Agrícolas, Ganaderas, Silvícolas y Pesqueras) regime under LISR Art. 74-75 provides significant tax benefits for primary sector activities, including income exemptions and simplified compliance.
- Individual exemptions are calculated at 40 times the annual UMA value (approximately MXN 1,584,254 for 2024 using the annual UMA of MXN 39,606.36). Corporate exemptions are capped at 20 UMA per partner, with a total maximum of 200 UMA per entity.
- Agricultural inputs (seeds, fertilizers, feed, pesticides, agricultural machinery) are subject to 0% IVA under LIVA Art. 2-A, which allows producers to claim full input IVA credits and request refunds.
- Ejido (communal land) income has special provisions recognizing the communal ownership structure. Ejidos can elect to register as legal entities and access the AGAPE regime benefits.
- IEPS on pesticides ranges from 6% to 9% based on toxicity classification (LISR Art. 2(I)(I) of the IEPS law): Category 1-2 toxicity at 9%, Category 3 at 7%, and Category 4 at 6%.
- Agricultural businesses with employees remain subject to PTU (10% profit sharing), though the AGAPE exemptions reduce the taxable profit base from which PTU is calculated.
- Simplified accounting (contabilidad simplificada) is available for agricultural taxpayers meeting revenue thresholds, reducing record-keeping and filing obligations.
