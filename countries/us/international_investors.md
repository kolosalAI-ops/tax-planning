# United States - International Investor Tax Guide

> **Jurisdiction**: Federal (IRS) / CFIUS
> **Governing Law**: IRC, Defense Production Act (DPA), FIRRMA 2018
> **Tax Year**: 2024/2025
> **Currency**: USD ($)

---

## 1. Foreign Ownership Restrictions & Investment Approvals

### 1.1 CFIUS Screening Framework

The Committee on Foreign Investment in the United States (CFIUS) is an interagency body chaired by the Secretary of the Treasury that reviews foreign acquisitions of, or investments in, US businesses for national security implications. CFIUS authority derives from Section 721 of the Defense Production Act (DPA) of 1950, as amended (50 USC 4565), and was substantially expanded by the Foreign Investment Risk Review Modernization Act of 2018 (FIRRMA, P.L. 115-232, Title XVII). Implementing regulations are codified at 31 CFR Parts 800-802.

| Filing Type | Trigger | Timeline | Penalty for Non-Compliance |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Mandatory Declaration** | Acquisition of ≥25% interest (≥49% for certain investors) in a TID US business involving a foreign government; critical technology requiring export license | **30 days** for assessment; then 45-day review if escalated | Up to the value of the transaction (50 USC §4565(b)(4)(D)) |
| **Voluntary Notice** | Any covered transaction not subject to mandatory filing | **45-day initial review** + **45-day investigation** if extended | No penalty for not filing, but CFIUS may impose retroactive conditions |
| **Short-Form Declaration** | Available for any covered transaction in lieu of full notice | **30-day assessment** period | Same as mandatory declaration |

**Key Definitions (31 CFR §800.248):**
- **TID US Business**: A US business involved in critical technology, critical infrastructure, or sensitive personal data
- **Covered Transaction**: Any transaction that could result in foreign control of a US business, or a covered investment by a foreign person in a TID US business
- **Covered Investment**: Non-controlling investment granting access to material nonpublic technical information, board membership/observer rights, or involvement in substantive decision-making (31 CFR §800.211)

**CFIUS Process Flow:**

| Stage | Duration | Outcome |
|---|---|---|
| Declaration/Notice filing | Day 0 | Process initiated |
| Initial review period | **45 calendar days** (notice) / **30 days** (declaration) | Cleared, escalated, or parties asked to file full notice |
| Investigation (if triggered) | Additional **45 calendar days** | Cleared, conditions imposed (mitigation agreement), or referred to President |
| Presidential decision | **15 calendar days** | Block, divest, or approve with conditions |

### 1.2 Sector-Specific Restrictions

The United States imposes statutory foreign ownership limitations in specific regulated sectors. These restrictions exist independently of CFIUS and are enforced by the relevant sector regulator.

| Sector | Foreign Ownership Cap | Governing Law | Regulator |
|---|---|---|---|
| Airlines | **25% voting interest** in US carriers | 49 USC §40102(a)(2), §41102 | Department of Transportation (DOT) |
| Broadcasting (radio/TV) | **25% voting interest** (direct); **25%** of parent company (indirect) | 47 USC §310(b)(3)-(4) (Communications Act) | Federal Communications Commission (FCC) |
| Banking (national banks) | **No blanket cap**, but ≥10% triggers Change in Bank Control Act notification; ≥25% triggers Bank Holding Company Act | 12 USC §1817(j) (CIBC Act); 12 USC §1841 (BHC Act) | OCC / Federal Reserve |
| Defense contracting | **Effective exclusion** for classified work via NISPOM; foreign ownership, control, or influence (FOCI) mitigation required | NISPOM (32 CFR Part 117); ITAR (22 CFR Parts 120-130) | DCSA / DDTC |
| Nuclear energy | **No foreign ownership, control, or domination** of NRC licensees | 42 USC §2133(d), 10 CFR §50.38 | Nuclear Regulatory Commission (NRC) |
| Shipping (Jones Act) | **75% US-citizen owned** for coastwise trade vessels; **100% US-built** | 46 USC §55102 (Jones Act); 46 USC §50501 | US Maritime Administration (MARAD) |
| Mining (federal lands) | Must be US citizen, association of US citizens, or **US-organized corporation** | 30 USC §22 (Mining Law of 1872) | Bureau of Land Management (BLM) |

**Exon-Florio Amendment (50 USC §4565(d)):** Authorizes the President to suspend or prohibit any acquisition, merger, or takeover of a US business by a foreign person when there is credible evidence that the foreign interest exercising control might take action threatening national security, and existing laws (other than IEEPA) are inadequate to protect national security.

### 1.3 Land & Real Property

The United States has **no general federal prohibition** on foreign ownership of real estate. However, specific disclosure and reporting requirements apply:

| Requirement | Details | Legal Basis |
|---|---|---|
| **AFIDA Reporting** | Foreign persons who acquire, dispose of, or hold an interest in US agricultural land must report to the Secretary of Agriculture within 90 days | Agricultural Foreign Investment Disclosure Act of 1978 (7 USC §3501-3508); 7 CFR Part 781 |
| **State-Level Farmland Restrictions** | Multiple states restrict or ban foreign ownership of agricultural land (e.g., Iowa, Minnesota, Wisconsin, North Dakota, Oklahoma) | Varies by state statute |
| **FIRPTA** | Dispositions of US real property interests by foreign persons are subject to taxation (see Section 2.1) | IRC §897, §1445 |
| **FinCEN GTO / Real Estate** | Geographic Targeting Orders require title insurance companies to report certain all-cash real estate purchases above thresholds | 31 USC §5326; FinCEN GTOs |

As of 2024, approximately **30 states** have some form of restriction on foreign ownership of agricultural land, ranging from outright prohibition to disclosure requirements.

### 1.4 Registration Requirements

Foreign-owned entities operating in the United States must satisfy several federal registration and reporting obligations:

| Requirement | Details | Legal Basis / Form |
|---|---|---|
| **Employer Identification Number (EIN)** | All foreign-owned US entities must obtain an EIN; foreign persons may also need an EIN for US tax withholding | IRC §6109; Form SS-4 |
| **FinCEN Beneficial Ownership Information (BOI)** | Reporting companies must file BOI reports identifying beneficial owners (≥25% ownership or substantial control); effective January 1, 2024 for new entities | Corporate Transparency Act (P.L. 116-283, §6403); 31 CFR Part 1010 |
| **Form 5472** | Required for any 25%-foreign-owned US corporation (or foreign corporation engaged in US trade/business) to report related-party transactions | IRC §6038A; Treas. Reg. §1.6038A-2 |
| **FIRPTA Certification** | Transferees must withhold 15% on dispositions of USRPI unless a FIRPTA affidavit (non-foreign affidavit) or withholding certificate is obtained | IRC §1445; Treas. Reg. §1.1445-2 |
| **Form W-8BEN-E** | Foreign entities must provide to US withholding agents to claim treaty benefits or establish foreign status | IRC §1441; Treas. Reg. §1.1441-1(e)(2) |
| **State Registration** | Foreign-owned entities must register in each state where they do business (foreign qualification) | Varies by state |

**Penalties for Non-Compliance:**
- Form 5472: **$25,000** per failure to file or maintain records (IRC §6038A(d))
- BOI Reports: **$500/day** civil penalty; criminal penalties up to **$10,000** and/or **2 years imprisonment** (31 USC §5336(h))

## 2. Non-Resident Investor Taxation

### 2.1 Capital Gains Tax on Disposal of Shares/Assets

The United States generally does **not** tax foreign persons on capital gains from the sale of personal property (including shares of stock), unless the gain is effectively connected with a US trade or business or relates to a US real property interest (USRPI).

| Asset Type | Tax Treatment for Non-Residents | Legal Basis |
|---|---|---|
| **Shares of non-USRPHC corporation** | **Not taxable** (unless ECI) | IRC §871(a)(2), §865(a) |
| **Shares of USRPHC** | Taxable as USRPI at **21%** corporate rate (or up to **37%** individual rate) on net gain | IRC §897(a), §897(c)(2) |
| **US real property** (direct ownership) | Taxable as USRPI; **15% withholding** on gross amount (or 10% if residence and amount realized ≤$1M) | IRC §897(a), §1445(a), §1445(c)(4) |
| **Partnership interests** (with US real property) | Taxable under FIRPTA; **15% withholding** on amount realized attributable to USRPI | IRC §897(g), §1446(f) |
| **ECI-connected gains** | Taxable at regular graduated rates (up to 21% corporate / 37% individual) | IRC §871(b), §882(a) |

**FIRPTA (Foreign Investment in Real Property Tax Act):**
- Enacted as IRC §897 and §1445
- A **US Real Property Holding Corporation (USRPHC)** is a corporation where the fair market value of its USRPIs equals or exceeds **50%** of the FMV of its total real property interests plus business assets (IRC §897(c)(2))
- The **cleansing rule**: If the stock is regularly traded on an established securities market, FIRPTA applies only if the foreign person held more than **5%** of the class of stock at any time during the shorter of the 5-year period ending on the disposition date or the holder's holding period (IRC §897(c)(3))
- **Withholding**: The transferee must withhold **15%** of the amount realized on the disposition of a USRPI (IRC §1445(a), increased from 10% to 15% by the PATH Act of 2015 for amounts realized exceeding $1,000,000)

### 2.2 Dividend Withholding Tax

The United States imposes a **30%** statutory withholding tax on dividends paid to non-resident aliens and foreign corporations (IRC §1441(a)-(b), §1442(a)). This rate is frequently reduced by bilateral tax treaties.

| Treaty Partner | Portfolio Dividends (%) | Direct Investment Dividends (%) | Ownership Threshold for Reduced Rate |
|---|---|---|---|
| **United Kingdom** | **15%** | **0%** | ≥80% of voting power |
| **Canada** | **15%** | **5%** | ≥10% of voting stock |
| **Japan** | **10%** | **5%** | ≥10% of voting stock (0% if ≥50% for 12 months) |
| **Germany** | **15%** | **5%** | ≥10% of voting stock (0% if ≥80%) |
| **Netherlands** | **15%** | **5%** | ≥10% of voting stock (0% if ≥80%) |
| **Australia** | **15%** | **5%** | ≥10% of voting stock |
| **India** | **25%** | **15%** | ≥10% of voting stock |
| **China** | **10%** | **10%** | N/A |
| **Singapore** | **15%** | **5%** | ≥10% of voting stock |
| **France** | **15%** | **5%** | ≥10% of voting stock (0% if ≥80%) |
| **South Korea** | **15%** | **10%** | ≥10% of voting stock |
| **Mexico** | **10%** | **5%** | ≥10% of voting stock |
| **Ireland** | **15%** | **5%** | ≥10% of voting stock |
| **Switzerland** | **15%** | **5%** | ≥10% of voting stock (0% if ≥80% + other conditions) |
| No treaty | **30%** | **30%** | N/A |

**Key Provisions:**
- IRC §894(a): Treaty provisions override IRC where applicable, subject to later-in-time rule
- Qualified dividends from treaty countries may be eligible for reduced individual rates for US residents, but this does not affect the withholding rate for non-residents
- The recipient must provide a valid **Form W-8BEN** (individuals) or **Form W-8BEN-E** (entities) to the withholding agent to claim treaty benefits
- IRC §1441(c)(9): Portfolio interest exemption does **not** apply to dividends

### 2.3 Branch Profits Tax

IRC §884 imposes a **30%** branch profits tax on the "dividend equivalent amount" of a foreign corporation's effectively connected earnings and profits, in addition to the regular corporate income tax on ECI. This tax is designed to equalize the treatment of foreign corporations operating through a US branch with those operating through a US subsidiary.

| Parameter | Value | Legal Basis |
|---|---|---|
| Branch profits tax rate | **30%** | IRC §884(a) |
| Applied to | Dividend equivalent amount (DEA) | IRC §884(b) |
| DEA calculation | ECI earnings & profits, adjusted for changes in US net equity | IRC §884(b)(2) |
| Treaty reduction | Typically reduced to **5%** (varies by treaty) | IRC §884(e) |
| Complete exemption | Some treaties exempt branch profits tax entirely (e.g., for qualified residents) | Treaty-specific |

**Effective Rate Comparison (Federal Only):**

| Structure | Income Tax | Additional Tax | Combined Effective Rate (Pre-Treaty) | Combined Effective Rate (5% Treaty BPT) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **US C-Corp Subsidiary** | 21% on taxable income | 30% WHT on dividends (on after-tax profits) | **44.7%** (21% + 30% x 79%) | **24.95%** (21% + 5% x 79%) |
| **US Branch** | 21% on ECI | 30% BPT on DEA (on after-tax earnings) | **44.7%** (21% + 30% x 79%) | **24.95%** (21% + 5% x 79%) |

The branch profits tax and dividend withholding tax are designed to produce equivalent results, so the choice between a branch and subsidiary is generally driven by non-tax factors and specific treaty provisions.

### 2.4 Exit Tax / Deemed Disposition

The United States does **not** impose a corporate-level exit tax on foreign investors ceasing to do business or divesting from the US. However, several anti-avoidance provisions apply:

| Provision | Description | Legal Basis |
|---|---|---|
| **IRC §877A** | Expatriation tax for individuals: covered expatriates are treated as having sold all worldwide property at FMV on the day before expatriation; net gain exceeding **$866,000** (2024, indexed) is taxable | IRC §877A(a), §877A(a)(3)(A) |
| **IRC §367(a)** | Outbound transfers of property to a foreign corporation in a reorganization or liquidation trigger gain recognition (no tax-free treatment) | IRC §367(a)(1) |
| **IRC §7874** | Anti-inversion rules: if a foreign corporation acquires substantially all assets of a US corporation and ≥**80%** of the new foreign parent's stock is held by former shareholders of the US entity, the foreign corporation is treated as a **domestic corporation** for US tax purposes | IRC §7874(b) |
| **IRC §7874 (60% test)** | If the former shareholders hold ≥**60%** but <80%, the inverted corporation cannot use tax attributes (e.g., NOLs, credits) to offset "inversion gain" for **10 years** | IRC §7874(a), §7874(d) |
| **IRC §4985** | **15%** excise tax on stock compensation of insiders of inverted corporations | IRC §4985(a) |

## 3. Repatriation of Profits

### 3.1 Dividend Payment Procedures

The United States imposes **no federal regulatory restrictions** on the payment of dividends by a US corporation to its foreign shareholders, beyond the applicable withholding tax obligations.

| Requirement | Details |
|---|---|
| Federal approval | **None required** |
| IRS pre-clearance | **Not required** |
| Corporate law solvency test | Dividends must comply with the state of incorporation's corporate law (e.g., DGCL §170 for Delaware: dividends payable from surplus or net profits) |
| Withholding obligation | Payor must withhold at statutory or treaty rate and remit to IRS via **Form 1042** (annual) and **Form 1042-S** (recipient statement) |
| Filing deadlines | Form 1042: **March 15** following the calendar year; Form 1042-S: **March 15** to recipients and IRS |

### 3.2 Foreign Exchange Controls

The United States maintains **no foreign exchange controls**. There are no restrictions on the amount of currency that can be transferred into or out of the country, and no government approval is required for cross-border payments.

| Parameter | Status |
|---|---|
| FX controls | **None** |
| Cross-border payment restrictions | **None** |
| Currency conversion restrictions | **None** |
| Central bank registration for FX transactions | **Not required** |
| OFAC sanctions compliance | **Required** - transactions with sanctioned countries, entities, or individuals are prohibited under the International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA, 50 USC §1701-1706) and the Trading with the Enemy Act (TWEA, 50 USC App. §1-44) |
| FinCEN reporting | CTR required for cash transactions exceeding **$10,000** (31 USC §5313); SARs for suspicious activity (31 USC §5318(g)) |

### 3.3 Loan Repayment, Royalties & Management Fee Repatriation

| Payment Type | Default WHT Rate | Common Treaty Rate | Key IRC Provision |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Interest** | **30%** | **0-15%** (many treaties provide 0% for arm's length interest) | IRC §1441(a), §1442(a) |
| **Royalties** | **30%** | **0-15%** (some treaties, e.g., UK/Canada, provide 0%) | IRC §1441(a), §1442(a) |
| **Management/service fees** | **30%** (if FDAP income) | **0-15%** (varies; many treaties exempt if no PE) | IRC §1441(a); treaty Business Profits article |
| **Rent** | **30%** (if not ECI) | Treaty-dependent | IRC §1441(a) |
| **Guarantor fees** | **30%** | Treaty-dependent | IRC §1441(a) |

**Portfolio Interest Exemption (IRC §871(h), §881(c)):**
- Interest paid on obligations in registered form to foreign persons is **exempt from US withholding tax** if:
  - The recipient is not a 10%-or-greater shareholder (IRC §871(h)(3))
  - The interest is not contingent interest (IRC §871(h)(4))
  - The recipient provides a valid **Form W-8BEN or W-8BEN-E**
  - The obligation is not a bank deposit

**Transfer Pricing (IRC §482):**
- All intercompany transactions must be conducted at **arm's length** prices
- The IRS may reallocate income between related parties if prices do not reflect arm's length standards
- Transfer pricing methods: Comparable Uncontrolled Price (CUP), Resale Price, Cost Plus, Comparable Profits Method (CPM), Profit Split (Treas. Reg. §1.482-3 through §1.482-6)

**Earnings Stripping (IRC §163(j)):**
- Business interest expense deduction is limited to **30% of Adjusted Taxable Income** (ATI)
- Disallowed interest carries forward indefinitely
- Small business exemption: average annual gross receipts ≤ **$29 million** (2024, indexed) for the 3 prior tax years

### 3.4 Regulatory Registration

| Requirement | Details |
|---|---|
| Central bank registration | **Not required** (the Federal Reserve does not regulate inbound/outbound capital flows) |
| Form W-8BEN-E | Foreign entities must provide to US withholding agents to claim treaty benefits and establish foreign status (Treas. Reg. §1.1441-1(e)(2)) |
| Form 1042-S | US withholding agents must report all payments of FDAP income to foreign persons, with copies to IRS and recipients |
| Form 8833 | Treaty-based return positions must be disclosed (IRC §6114; Treas. Reg. §301.6114-1) |
| FATCA (IRC §1471-1474) | Foreign financial institutions must report US account holders; foreign entities must certify beneficial ownership or face **30%** withholding |

## 4. Branch vs. Subsidiary Considerations

### 4.1 Structural Options

Foreign investors entering the US market typically choose among the following entity structures:

| Structure | Entity Type | Federal Tax Treatment | Key Characteristics |
|---|---|---|---|
| **C-Corporation Subsidiary** | Inc. / Corp. | Taxed at **21%** corporate rate; dividends to foreign parent subject to **30%** WHT (or treaty rate) | Separate legal entity; limited liability; most common for substantial operations |
| **LLC (default classification)** | LLC | Disregarded entity (single member) or partnership (multi-member); income flows through to foreign owner | Check-the-box election available (Treas. Reg. §301.7701-3); foreign-owned SMLLC must file Form 5472 and pro forma 1120 |
| **LLC (elected C-Corp)** | LLC | Taxed as C-Corp if election made | Combines LLC flexibility with corporate tax treatment |
| **Branch / PE** | No separate entity | Foreign corp taxed at **21%** on ECI + **30%** BPT | No entity formation; lower setup costs; unlimited liability for parent |
| **Partnership** | LP / LLP | Pass-through; partners taxed on distributive share; ECI withholding at **21%** (corporate) or **37%** (individual) on foreign partner's share | IRC §864(c)(8): gain on sale of partnership interest attributable to ECI is taxable; withholding under §1446(a) and §1446(f) |

**Check-the-Box Elections (Treas. Reg. §301.7701-3):**
- An eligible entity may elect to be classified as a corporation or as a partnership/disregarded entity
- Election made on **Form 8832**, effective on the date specified (but no more than 75 days before or 12 months after filing)
- Once elected, a change cannot be made for **60 months** (Treas. Reg. §301.7701-3(c)(1)(iv))

### 4.2 Tax Treatment Differences

| Factor | C-Corp Subsidiary | Branch (PE) |
|---|---|---|
| **Income tax rate** | **21%** (IRC §11(b)) | **21%** on ECI (IRC §882(a)) |
| **Additional tax on repatriation** | **30%** dividend WHT (IRC §1442) or treaty rate | **30%** branch profits tax (IRC §884) or treaty rate |
| **Combined pre-treaty rate** | **44.7%** | **44.7%** |
| **Combined with typical 5% treaty rate** | **24.95%** | **24.95%** |
| **Loss utilization** | Losses trapped in US subsidiary | Losses may offset foreign parent's income (depending on home country rules) |
| **Transfer pricing exposure** | Full IRC §482 scrutiny on intercompany transactions | Internal dealings may also be scrutinized under treaty PE attribution rules |
| **State tax** | Subject to state CIT where nexus exists | Subject to state CIT where ECI is earned |
| **Liability** | Limited to subsidiary's assets | Parent has unlimited liability for branch obligations |

**Effective Combined Rate Illustration (Assuming $1,000 Pre-Tax Income):**

| Scenario | Subsidiary (No Treaty) | Subsidiary (5% Treaty WHT) | Branch (No Treaty) | Branch (5% Treaty BPT) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Pre-tax income | $1,000 | $1,000 | $1,000 | $1,000 |
| Federal CIT (21%) | ($210) | ($210) | ($210) | ($210) |
| After-tax profit | $790 | $790 | $790 | $790 |
| WHT / BPT | ($237) | ($39.50) | ($237) | ($39.50) |
| **Net to investor** | **$553** | **$750.50** | **$553** | **$750.50** |
| **Effective total rate** | **44.7%** | **24.95%** | **44.7%** | **24.95%** |

### 4.3 PE Rules

The concept of permanent establishment (PE) determines when a foreign enterprise's business profits are taxable in the United States under an applicable tax treaty.

| PE Trigger | Description | Typical Treaty Threshold |
|---|---|---|
| **Fixed place of business** | Office, branch, factory, workshop, mine, oil well, quarry | Presence alone is sufficient (US Model Treaty Art. 5(1)-(2)) |
| **Construction PE** | Building site or construction/installation project | **12 months** (US Model Treaty Art. 5(3)); some treaties specify 6 months |
| **Service PE** | Performance of services by employees | **Not included** in most US treaties; some newer treaties include 183-day service PE |
| **Dependent agent** | Person habitually exercising authority to conclude contracts in the name of the enterprise | Creates PE (US Model Treaty Art. 5(5)) |
| **Independent agent** | Broker, commission agent, or other independent status acting in ordinary course | Does **not** create PE (US Model Treaty Art. 5(6)) |
| **Preparatory/auxiliary** | Activities solely of preparatory or auxiliary character (e.g., storage, display, purchasing, information gathering) | Excluded from PE (US Model Treaty Art. 5(4)) |

**Domestic Law (No Treaty Applies):**
- IRC §864(b): defines "trade or business within the United States"
- IRC §864(c): determines which income is "effectively connected" with a US trade or business
- The "trade or business" standard under domestic law is generally broader (lower threshold) than the treaty PE standard
- A foreign corporation with a US trade or business but no PE under an applicable treaty may use the treaty to shield business profits from US taxation

### 4.4 Compliance Differences

| Requirement | C-Corp Subsidiary | Branch (PE) |
|---|---|---|
| **Federal income tax return** | **Form 1120** (US Corporation Income Tax Return) | **Form 1120-F** (US Income Tax Return of a Foreign Corporation) |
| **Related-party reporting** | **Form 5472** (for 25%-foreign-owned corporations) | Form 5472 also required if engaged in reportable transactions |
| **Transfer pricing documentation** | Required under IRC §6662(e); contemporaneous documentation to avoid penalties | Same requirements apply |
| **Treaty-based return position** | **Form 8833** (if claiming treaty benefits) | **Form 8833** (if claiming treaty benefits, e.g., PE threshold protection) |
| **State tax filings** | Required in each state where nexus exists | Required in each state where ECI is earned |
| **FATCA/CRS reporting** | US entity; FATCA applies to payments made by entity | Foreign parent may have FATCA obligations |
| **BOI reporting** | Required under Corporate Transparency Act | May apply if a reporting company is involved |
| **Annual franchise/registered agent fees** | State of incorporation + states of qualification | Typically less registration, but may still need state registration |

## 5. Foreign Tax Credit Mechanisms

### 5.1 Unilateral Relief

The United States provides relief from double taxation primarily through the **foreign tax credit** (FTC) system under IRC §901-909. US taxpayers (including US subsidiaries of foreign parents) may elect to credit foreign income taxes against their US tax liability, or alternatively deduct them as a business expense.

| Parameter | Value | Legal Basis |
|---|---|---|
| **Direct FTC** | Credit for foreign income taxes paid or accrued directly by the US taxpayer | IRC §901(b)(1) |
| **Deemed-paid FTC** | Credit for foreign taxes deemed paid through CFC inclusions (Subpart F, GILTI) | IRC §960(a)-(d) |
| **Election** | Taxpayer may elect credit or deduction (election applies to **all** foreign taxes for the year) | IRC §901(a), §275(a)(4) |
| **Creditability test** | Foreign levy must be a tax; must be compulsory; must be an income tax in the US sense (net income, realization, gross receipts) | Treas. Reg. §1.901-2(a), §1.901-2(b) (2022 final regulations) |

### 5.2 Credit Limitations & Baskets

The FTC is subject to a per-category limitation to prevent foreign taxes on high-taxed foreign income from offsetting US tax on low-taxed or domestic income.

**IRC §904 Limitation Formula:**

FTC Limitation = US Tax Liability x (Foreign Source Taxable Income in Category / Worldwide Taxable Income)

| Basket Category | Description | Legal Basis |
|---|---|---|
| **General category** | Active business income, including most foreign branch income prior to 2018 | IRC §904(d)(1)(B) |
| **Passive category** | Passive income (dividends, interest, rents, royalties, gains from passive assets) | IRC §904(d)(1)(A) |
| **GILTI** | Global Intangible Low-Taxed Income inclusions | IRC §904(d)(1)(A) (treated as separate basket per §904(d)(1) flush language) |
| **Foreign branch** | Income attributable to foreign branches of US persons | IRC §904(d)(1)(B) (separate basket post-TCJA) |
| **Section 901(j)** | Income from sanctioned countries | IRC §901(j) |
| **Treaty-resourced** | Income resourced under treaty provisions | IRC §904(d)(6) |

**Source Rules (IRC §861-865):** The determination of whether income is US-source or foreign-source is critical to the FTC limitation calculation. Key source rules include:
- Dividends: source based on residence of payor (IRC §861(a)(2))
- Interest: source based on residence of payor (IRC §861(a)(1))
- Royalties: source based on where property is used (IRC §861(a)(4))
- Services: source based on where services are performed (IRC §861(a)(3), §862(a)(3))
- Sales of personal property: source based on residence of seller (IRC §865(a)), with exceptions for inventory (title passage) and depreciable property

### 5.3 Carry-Forward / Carry-Back

| Parameter | Value | Legal Basis |
|---|---|---|
| **Carry-forward** | **10 years** | IRC §904(c) |
| **Carry-back** | **1 year** | IRC §904(c) |
| **GILTI basket** | **No carry-forward or carry-back** (single-year computation) | IRC §904(c) (as applied; GILTI FTC is computed annually with no excess credit carryover) |
| **Order of application** | Current year credits used first; then carry-forward credits in chronological order; then carry-back | IRC §904(c) |
| **AMT FTC** | Separate FTC computation for corporate AMT (IRC §59(a)) | IRC §59(a) |

## 6. CFC Rules Impact on International Investors

### 6.1 Subpart F Income (IRC §951-964)

The Subpart F regime requires US shareholders of Controlled Foreign Corporations (CFCs) to include certain categories of the CFC's income in their gross income currently, regardless of whether the income is distributed.

| Parameter | Value | Legal Basis |
|---|---|---|
| **CFC definition** | A foreign corporation in which **US shareholders** (each owning ≥**10%** of vote or value) own more than **50%** of vote or value | IRC §957(a), §951(b) |
| **US shareholder** | US person owning ≥**10%** of total combined voting power or value | IRC §951(b) (expanded by TCJA to include value) |
| **Subpart F categories** | Foreign Personal Holding Company Income (FPHCI), Foreign Base Company Sales Income, Foreign Base Company Services Income, insurance income, international boycott income, bribes | IRC §952(a), §954 |
| **FPHCI** | Dividends, interest, rents, royalties, gains from property transactions (with exceptions) | IRC §954(c) |
| **De minimis rule** | If Subpart F income is less than the lesser of **5%** of gross income or **$1,000,000**, no Subpart F income is included | IRC §954(b)(3)(A) |
| **Full inclusion rule** | If Subpart F income exceeds **70%** of gross income, **all** gross income is treated as Subpart F income | IRC §954(b)(3)(B) |
| **Same-country exception** | Rents/royalties received from related CFCs organized in the same country are excluded from FPHCI | IRC §954(c)(3) |
| **Active rents/royalties** | Excluded from FPHCI if derived in the active conduct of a trade or business and received from unrelated persons | IRC §954(c)(2)(A) |

### 6.2 GILTI (IRC §951A)

The Global Intangible Low-Taxed Income regime, enacted by the TCJA effective for tax years of CFCs beginning after December 31, 2017, requires US shareholders of CFCs to include their pro rata share of the CFC's "tested income" exceeding a **10%** deemed return on qualified business asset investment (QBAI).

| Parameter | Value | Legal Basis |
|---|---|---|
| **Inclusion** | 100% of GILTI included in US shareholder's gross income | IRC §951A(a) |
| **GILTI formula** | Net CFC tested income minus (10% x QBAI minus specified interest expense) | IRC §951A(b) |
| **§250 deduction** | **50%** of GILTI inclusion (effective rate: **10.5%**) | IRC §250(a)(1)(B) |
| **Post-2025 deduction** | **37.5%** (effective rate: **13.125%**) | IRC §250(a)(3)(B) |
| **FTC on GILTI** | Limited to **80%** of foreign taxes paid or accrued on tested income | IRC §960(d)(1) |
| **GILTI FTC basket** | Separate basket; no carry-forward or carry-back of excess credits | IRC §904(d)(1)(A) |
| **QBAI** | Aggregate adjusted bases of specified tangible property used in CFC's trade or business (determined using ADS depreciation) | IRC §951A(d) |
| **Tested income** | Gross income of CFC excluding: ECI, Subpart F income, high-taxed exclusion income, dividends from related CFCs, certain foreign oil/gas income | IRC §951A(c)(2) |

**High-Tax Exclusion (Treas. Reg. §1.951A-2(c)(7)):**
- A CFC shareholder may elect to exclude GILTI tested income that is subject to an effective foreign tax rate exceeding **18.9%** (90% of the 21% US rate)
- The election is made annually and applies to all CFCs of the US shareholder
- Excluded income is instead tested under the Subpart F high-tax exception (IRC §954(b)(4))

### 6.3 Substance Requirements

| Parameter | Details |
|---|---|
| **Specific substance safe harbor** | The US does **not** have a formal substance safe harbor for CFC purposes (unlike some jurisdictions) |
| **Active business operations test** | Subpart F exceptions for sales and services income require activities to be performed **outside** the CFC's country of incorporation and outside the US; manufacturing exception requires substantial transformation |
| **Same-country exception** | Available for certain categories of FPHCI when payor and payee CFCs are in the same country (IRC §954(c)(3)) |
| **High-tax exclusion** | Available when effective foreign tax rate ≥ **18.9%** (Treas. Reg. §1.951A-2(c)(7) for GILTI; IRC §954(b)(4) for Subpart F) |
| **Check-the-box planning** | Hybrid entities may be used to reduce Subpart F income through disregarded payments between related CFCs | Treas. Reg. §301.7701-3 |
| **Anti-abuse rules** | IRC §956: investments in US property by CFCs may trigger deemed dividends; §959: previously taxed income (PTI) not taxed again when distributed | IRC §956, §959 |

### 7.2 LOB / PPT Anti-Avoidance

US tax treaties employ **Limitation on Benefits (LOB)** provisions as the primary anti-treaty-shopping mechanism. Unlike many OECD-aligned treaties, US treaties generally do **not** include a Principal Purpose Test (PPT).

| LOB Test | Description | Legal Basis |
|---|---|---|
| **Publicly traded company** | Shares are regularly traded on a recognized stock exchange | Treaty LOB article (varies by treaty) |
| **Ownership and base erosion** | ≥50% owned by qualifying residents; <50% of income paid/accrued to non-qualifying persons | Treaty LOB article |
| **Active trade or business** | Substantial, genuine active conduct of a trade or business in the treaty country | Treaty LOB article |
| **Derivative benefits** | Equivalent beneficiaries test: each owner would have been entitled to the same or better treaty benefits | Treaty LOB article |
| **Headquarters company** | Serves as headquarters for a multinational corporate group with substantial operations | Treaty LOB article (in some treaties) |
| **Competent authority discretion** | Resident may request a determination from the competent authority | Treaty LOB article |

**Key US-Specific Provisions:**
- IRC §894(c): Hybrid entity rule - treaty benefits denied for payments to/through fiscally transparent entities unless the relevant treaty partner taxes the item
- **Anti-conduit regulations** (Treas. Reg. §1.881-3): IRS may disregard an intermediate entity in a financing arrangement if used principally to obtain treaty benefits
- **Triangular provisions**: Some US treaties contain special provisions addressing "triangular" cases where a third-country PE of a treaty resident receives US-source income
- US treaties generally **do not** include the OECD Multilateral Instrument (MLI) changes, as the US has not signed the MLI

## 8. Permanent Establishment Risk

### 8.1 PE Definition & Thresholds

Under US domestic law, the relevant concept is "engaged in a trade or business within the United States" (IRC §864(b)-(c)), which is broader than the treaty PE concept. When a treaty applies, the PE definition in the treaty governs whether the US may tax business profits.

| PE Element | US Domestic Law | US Model Treaty (Art. 5) |
|---|---|---|
| **General threshold** | "Trade or business" — a lower threshold based on regularity, continuity, and profit motive | Fixed place of business through which the business is wholly or partly carried on |
| **Specific examples** | Any regular, continuous, and substantial business activity in the US | Office, branch, factory, workshop, mine, oil/gas well, quarry |
| **Construction** | No specific duration threshold under domestic law | **12 months** (US Model Treaty Art. 5(3)); some treaties specify 6 or 9 months |
| **Services** | Provision of services may constitute trade or business with minimal activity | **Not a PE trigger** in most US treaties; some newer treaties include 183-day service PE |
| **Preparatory/auxiliary** | No specific exclusion under domestic law (but may not rise to "trade or business") | Excluded: storage, display, purchasing, information gathering, preparatory/auxiliary activities (Art. 5(4)) |
| **Partner in US partnership** | Generally treated as engaged in US trade or business (IRC §875) | Treaty PE analysis applies to the partnership's activities |

**Key Domestic Law Provisions:**
- IRC §864(b)(1): Trading in stocks/securities safe harbor (foreign person trading through US broker/dealer is **not** treated as US trade or business)
- IRC §864(b)(2): Trading in commodities safe harbor (similar to securities safe harbor)
- IRC §864(c)(1)(A): Effectively connected income from assets — income from assets used in or held for use in US trade or business
- IRC §864(c)(1)(B): Effectively connected income from other factors — FDAP income if asset is used in US trade or business or activities are a material factor in realization

### 8.2 Agent PE & Digital PE

| Agent Type | PE Consequence | Legal Basis |
|---|---|---|
| **Dependent agent** (habitually exercises authority to conclude contracts in the enterprise's name) | **Creates PE** | US Model Treaty Art. 5(5); IRC §864(c) (ECI analysis) |
| **Independent agent** (acts in ordinary course of own business) | **Does not create PE** | US Model Treaty Art. 5(6) |
| **Commissionnaire arrangements** | Under domestic law, may create trade or business; under treaty, generally **not** PE if agent does not formally conclude contracts | Treaty Art. 5(5)-(6); Treas. Reg. §1.864-7 |
| **Digital PE** | **Not recognized** in US domestic law or US tax treaties; no digital services tax at federal level | No applicable provision |
| **Economic nexus (state level)** | Post-*South Dakota v. Wayfair* (2018), states may impose sales tax obligations based on economic presence ($100K sales / 200 transactions); income tax nexus also expanding through factor-presence and market-based sourcing | *South Dakota v. Wayfair, Inc.*, 585 U.S. 162 (2018); state statutes vary |

**State-Level Considerations:**
- Many states have adopted economic nexus standards for corporate income tax
- Typical state income tax nexus thresholds: **$500,000** in sales, **$50,000** in payroll, or **$50,000** in property in the state
- **P.L. 86-272** (15 USC §381-384): Provides protection from state income tax when the only activity is solicitation of orders for tangible personal property (does **not** apply to services, intangibles, or digital goods)
- Some states have adopted factor-presence nexus: bright-line thresholds for property, payroll, or sales
