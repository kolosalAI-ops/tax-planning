# Australia - International Investors

> **Jurisdiction**: Australian Taxation Office (ATO), Foreign Investment Review Board (FIRB), Australian Securities & Investments Commission (ASIC)
> **Governing Law**: Foreign Acquisitions and Takeovers Act 1975 (FATA), Income Tax Assessment Act 1997 (ITAA 1997), Income Tax Assessment Act 1936 (ITAA 1936), Corporations Act 2001
> **Tax Year**: 2024-25 (July 1, 2024 - June 30, 2025)
> **Currency**: Australian Dollar (AUD / A$)

---

## 1. Foreign Ownership Restrictions & Investment Approvals

> **Legal Basis**: Foreign Acquisitions and Takeovers Act 1975 (FATA); Foreign Acquisitions and Takeovers Fees Imposition Act 2015; Foreign Investment Reform (Protecting Australia's National Security) Act 2020

### Foreign Investment Review Board (FIRB)

The **FIRB** is a non-statutory advisory body that examines proposals by foreign persons to invest in Australia and makes recommendations to the **Treasurer**, who holds the ultimate decision-making power under the FATA. FIRB does not itself approve or reject applications -- it advises the Treasurer on whether proposals are contrary to the national interest or national security.

### Screening Thresholds (2024)

Foreign investment proposals must be notified to FIRB and receive approval before proceeding if they meet or exceed the applicable monetary threshold. Certain categories are screened at **AUD 0** (all transactions require approval regardless of value).

| Investment Category | Screening Threshold (AUD) | Legal Reference |
|---|---|---|
| **Business acquisitions (agreement country investors)** | **$310 million** | FATA 1975 §47; Foreign Acquisitions and Takeovers Regulation 2015, reg 52 |
| **Business acquisitions (non-agreement country investors)** | **$289 million** | FATA 1975 §47; Regulation 2015, reg 50 |
| **Agricultural land** | **$15 million** (cumulative) | FATA 1975 §55-56A; Regulation 2015, reg 37 |
| **Agribusiness** | **$67 million** | FATA 1975 §47; Regulation 2015, reg 53 |
| **Residential land** | **$0** (all purchases screened) | FATA 1975 §56; Regulation 2015, Part 2, Division 3 |
| **Sensitive sectors** (media, telecom, transport, defence, encryption) | **$0** (all screened) | FATA 1975 §47; Regulation 2015, reg 51 (sensitive business) |
| **National security** (critical infrastructure, data, defence supply chain) | **$0** (all screened; call-in power) | FATA 1975 Part 3A (national security review); §66A-66C (call-in/last resort powers) |
| **Foreign government investors** | **$0** (all screened regardless of value) | FATA 1975 §56(2); Regulation 2015, reg 36 |

> **Agreement countries** include the United States, New Zealand, Japan, South Korea, China, Chile, Singapore, Thailand, and other FTA partners with investment chapters that provide higher thresholds.

### Key Statutory Provisions

| Provision | Description |
|---|---|
| **FATA 1975 §47** | Defines "significant actions" requiring notification -- acquiring substantial interests in entities, starting Australian businesses |
| **FATA 1975 §55-56A** | Regulates acquisitions of interests in Australian land, including agricultural land and residential land |
| **FATA 1975 Part 3A** | National security review framework (introduced 2021): Treasurer may review and prohibit any action on national security grounds |
| **FATA 1975 §66A-66C** | Call-in and last resort powers: Treasurer can review previously approved or non-notifiable actions if national security concerns arise |
| **FATA 1975 §98-98F** | Penalties for non-compliance: civil and criminal penalties, divestiture orders |

### Vacant Residential Land

Foreign investors who purchase residential land and leave it **vacant** (not genuinely occupied or available for rent for at least 183 days per year) are subject to an **annual vacancy fee** equal to the original FIRB application fee. This fee is imposed under the Foreign Acquisitions and Takeovers Fees Imposition Act 2015 and enforced by the ATO.

### Application Fees

| Investment Value (AUD) | Fee |
|---|---|
| $0 - $75,000 (established residential) | Varied schedule starting at ~$14,100 |
| Business/agribusiness (up to $50M) | $14,100 |
| Business/agribusiness ($50M - $100M) | $28,200 |
| >$2 billion | $1,045,000+ |

## 2. Non-Resident Investor Taxation

> **Legal Basis**: ITAA 1997 Division 855 (CGT for foreign residents); ITAA 1936 Division 11A (Withholding tax on dividends, interest, royalties); ITAA 1997 Division 275 (Managed Investment Trusts)

### Capital Gains Tax -- Taxable Australian Property (TAP)

Under **ITAA 1997 Division 855**, non-residents are generally **exempt** from Australian CGT except on disposals of **Taxable Australian Property (TAP)**. This represents a territorial approach to capital gains for non-residents.

| TAP Category | Description | Legal Reference |
|---|---|---|
| **Direct real property interests** | Interests in Australian land and buildings | ITAA 1997 §855-20(1)(a) |
| **Mining rights** | Rights to minerals, quarry materials, petroleum | ITAA 1997 §855-20(1)(b) |
| **Assets of Australian PE** | Assets used in carrying on a business through an Australian permanent establishment | ITAA 1997 §855-20(1)(c) |
| **Indirect real property interests** | Non-portfolio interest (>=10%) in an entity where >50% of market value is attributable to Australian real property | ITAA 1997 §855-25 (principal asset test) |
| **Options/rights over TAP** | Options or rights to acquire any of the above | ITAA 1997 §855-20(1)(d) |

> **Non-portfolio interest**: A membership interest of **10% or more** in the entity (ITAA 1997 §855-25). Portfolio interests (<10%) are exempt from the indirect real property test.

### CGT Withholding Regime

| Parameter | Value | Legal Reference |
|---|---|---|
| Withholding rate | **12.5%** of purchase price | ITAA Sch 1 §14-200 |
| Threshold | Assets with market value of **AUD 750,000** or more | ITAA Sch 1 §14-200(3)(b) |
| Applies to | Purchasers acquiring relevant CGT assets (typically real property) from any vendor, unless clearance certificate obtained | ITAA Sch 1 §14-200 to §14-250 |
| Clearance certificate | Australian resident vendors can obtain a clearance certificate to avoid withholding | ITAA Sch 1 §14-220 |
| Variation | Non-residents can apply for a variation to reduce withholding if actual CGT liability is lower | |

### Dividend Withholding Tax

| Dividend Type | WHT Rate | Legal Reference |
|---|---|---|
| **Fully franked dividends** | **0%** (imputation credits eliminate WHT) | ITAA 1936 §128B(3)(ga) |
| **Unfranked dividends** | **30%** (default) | ITAA 1936 §128B(1) |
| **Unfranked dividends (treaty-reduced)** | **5-15%** depending on treaty and shareholding | ITAA 1936 Division 11A; applicable DTA |
| **Conduit foreign income (CFI)** | **0%** (WHT-free distribution to non-residents) | ITAA 1997 Division 802 |

> **Conduit Foreign Income (Division 802)**: Where an Australian company earns foreign income that has not been subject to Australian tax, it may distribute that income to non-resident shareholders as CFI, which is **exempt from dividend WHT**. This makes Australia attractive as a regional holding company jurisdiction.

### Managed Investment Trust (MIT) Withholding

| Parameter | Value | Legal Reference |
|---|---|---|
| MIT WHT rate (treaty country residents) | **15%** final withholding | ITAA 1997 Division 275 |
| MIT WHT rate (non-treaty country residents) | **30%** | ITAA 1997 Division 275 |
| Qualifying MIT | Widely held, not closely held; registered managed investment scheme | ITAA 1997 §275-10 |
| Attribution MIT (AMIT) | Elective regime with flow-through taxation and member-level adjustments | ITAA 1997 Division 276 |

### Branch Profits Tax

Australia imposes **no separate branch profits tax**. Branch income of a foreign corporation is taxed at the standard corporate rate of **30%** on Australian-source income. Remittance of branch profits to the head office is **not subject to additional tax**.

| **United Kingdom** | 0% / 15% (0% if >=10% holding under MLI/new treaty) | 0% | 5% | Australia-UK DTA 2003 |
| **Japan** | 0% / 10% (5% if >=10% holding) | 10% | 5% | Australia-Japan DTA 2008 |

## 3. Repatriation of Profits

> **Legal Basis**: ITAA 1936 Division 11A (WHT); Reserve Bank of Australia Act 1959; ITAA 1997 Division 802 (Conduit foreign income)

### Foreign Exchange Controls

Australia has **no foreign exchange controls**. The Australian dollar (AUD) is a **fully convertible currency**. There are no restrictions on the purchase or sale of foreign currencies, and no central bank registration or approval is required for international transfers.

| Parameter | Status |
|---|---|
| Currency convertibility | **Fully convertible** -- no restrictions |
| Central bank registration for remittances | **Not required** |
| Restrictions on dividend payments to non-residents | **None** (subject to WHT) |
| Restrictions on interest payments to non-residents | **None** (subject to WHT) |
| Restrictions on royalty payments to non-residents | **None** (subject to WHT) |
| Financial transaction tax (e.g., IOF) | **None** -- no tax on international transfers |
| Anti-money laundering reporting | Required for transactions >= AUD 10,000 (AUSTRAC) |

### Withholding on Repatriated Income

| Payment Type | Default WHT Rate | Treaty-Reduced Rate (Typical) | Legal Reference |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Dividends (franked)** | **0%** | 0% | ITAA 1936 §128B(3)(ga) |
| **Dividends (unfranked)** | **30%** | 5-15% | ITAA 1936 §128B(1); applicable DTA |
| **Interest** | **10%** | 0-10% (e.g., 0% for certain US treaty interest) | ITAA 1936 §128B(2); applicable DTA |
| **Royalties** | **30%** | 5-10% (typically) | ITAA 1936 §128B(2B); applicable DTA |
| **Technical service fees** | Not subject to separate WHT (not royalties unless know-how) | N/A | ITAA 1936 Division 11A |
| **Management fees** | Not subject to WHT (ordinary income rules apply) | N/A | General principles |
| **Branch profit remittances** | **0%** (no branch profits tax) | 0% | N/A |

### Conduit Foreign Income (Division 802)

The **conduit foreign income** provisions allow Australian companies to distribute foreign-source income to non-resident shareholders **free of dividend WHT**. This is particularly beneficial for Australian holding companies with foreign subsidiaries:

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| Eligible income | Foreign income not included in the company's assessable income in Australia, or NANE income from foreign sources |
| CFI account | Company maintains a conduit foreign income account (like a franking account for foreign income) |
| Distribution | Non-resident shareholders receiving CFI are **exempt from Australian dividend WHT** |
| Strategic use | Makes Australia viable as a **regional holding company** for Asia-Pacific operations |

## 4. Branch vs. Subsidiary

> **Legal Basis**: ITAA 1997 Section 4-10 (Income tax); Corporations Act 2001 Part 5B.2 (Registration of foreign companies); ITAA 1936 §6(1) (PE definition)

### Branch (Foreign Company Registered in Australia)

| Parameter | Detail | Legal Reference |
|---|---|---|
| Registration | Must register with ASIC as a foreign company carrying on business in Australia | Corporations Act 2001 Part 5B.2, §601CD |
| Tax rate on Australian-source income | **30%** | ITAA 1997 §23-5 |
| Branch profits tax | **None** -- no second-level tax on remittance of profits | N/A |
| Effective total tax rate | **30%** (single level) | |
| Financial reporting | Must lodge financial statements with ASIC (limited relief available for some categories) | Corporations Act 2001 §601CK |
| Deductions | Can deduct expenses incurred in deriving Australian income; arm's length allocation required | ITAA 1997 §8-1; Division 815 (transfer pricing) |
| Loss utilisation | Branch losses carry forward under same rules as companies (COT/BCT tests apply to head office entity) | ITAA 1997 Division 36 |

### Subsidiary (Australian-Incorporated Company)

| Parameter | Detail | Legal Reference |
|---|---|---|
| Tax rate | **30%** (general) or **25%** (base rate entity, turnover < $50M) | ITAA 1997 §23-5, §23-10 |
| Dividend WHT on unfranked dividends | **30%** (default); treaty-reduced | ITAA 1936 §128B |
| Dividend WHT on franked dividends | **0%** | ITAA 1936 §128B(3)(ga) |
| Effective total tax rate (unfranked) | **51%** (30% CIT + 30% WHT on remainder) or treaty-reduced | |
| Effective total tax rate (franked) | **30%** (0% WHT on franked dividends eliminates double tax) | |
| Imputation benefits | Subsidiary can frank dividends, providing credits to shareholders; **eliminates WHT on franked dividends** | ITAA 1997 Division 202 |
| Loss utilisation | Carry forward indefinitely; group consolidation available | ITAA 1997 Division 36; Part 3-90 (consolidation) |
| Tax consolidation | Available -- single entity treatment for wholly-owned groups | ITAA 1997 Part 3-90 |

### Comparison Table

| Factor | Branch | Subsidiary |
|---|---|---|
| **Tax on operating income** | 30% | 30% (or 25% BRE) |
| **Second-level tax** | 0% (no BPT) | 0% (franked) / 30% (unfranked, default) |
| **Effective total rate** | 30% | 30% (franked) / up to 51% (unfranked) |
| **Franking credits** | Not available | Available -- key advantage |
| **Limited liability** | No -- head office liable | Yes -- separate legal entity |
| **PE implications** | Constitutes PE by definition | Subsidiary alone is not a PE of parent |
| **Tax consolidation** | Not available | Available for wholly-owned groups |
| **CFI benefit** | Not available | Available (Division 802) |
| **Repatriation flexibility** | Direct remittance | Dividend declaration required |

### Hybrid Entity Considerations

| Entity Type | Treatment | Typical Use |
|---|---|---|
| **Partnership** | Flow-through taxation; partners taxed individually | Joint ventures, professional services |
| **Trust** | Flow-through if income distributed; otherwise taxed at 47% (top marginal rate) for companies at 30% | Property investment, fund management |
| **Managed Investment Trust (MIT)** | 15% final WHT for treaty residents; 30% non-treaty | Portfolio/fund investments by non-residents |
| **Joint Venture (unincorporated)** | Each JV participant taxed on their share | Mining, energy, construction |

## 5. Foreign Tax Credit (FITO)

> **Legal Basis**: ITAA 1997 Division 770 (Foreign income tax offset)

### Overview

Australia provides a **credit method** for relief from international double taxation through the **Foreign Income Tax Offset (FITO)**. Australian residents (including companies) who pay foreign income tax on foreign-sourced income included in their Australian assessable income may claim a FITO.

### FITO Rules

| Parameter | Detail | Legal Reference |
|---|---|---|
| **Eligibility** | Foreign income tax paid on income included in Australian assessable income | ITAA 1997 §770-10 |
| **Credit type** | Direct credit (taxes paid by the taxpayer); underlying credits for dividends from 10%+ held foreign companies (pre-2004 income years, now limited) | ITAA 1997 §770-10, §770-15 |
| **Limitation** | FITO cannot exceed the Australian tax payable on the foreign income | ITAA 1997 §770-75 |
| **Basket approach** | **One basket** -- all foreign income and taxes aggregated for the limitation calculation | ITAA 1997 §770-75 |
| **Carry-forward** | **Not available** (excess FITO is lost -- cannot be carried to future years; post-2008 reform) | ITAA 1997 §770-75; Tax Laws Amendment (2007 Measures No. 4) Act 2007 |
| **Carry-back** | **Not available** | N/A |
| **$1,000 de minimis** | If total foreign income tax paid <= $1,000, the offset is allowed without calculating the limit | ITAA 1997 §770-75(2) |

### FITO Calculation Methodology

| Step | Description |
|---|---|
| **Step 1** | Identify foreign income included in Australian assessable income |
| **Step 2** | Identify foreign income tax paid on that income (must be a tax on income, not other levies) |
| **Step 3** | Calculate FITO limit: (Foreign income / Total assessable income) x Australian tax payable |
| **Step 4** | FITO allowed = lesser of (a) foreign tax paid and (b) FITO limit |
| **Step 5** | Excess foreign tax (if any) is **permanently lost** -- no carry-forward or carry-back |

### FITO Limitations Table

| Scenario | Australian Tax Rate | Foreign Tax Rate | FITO Allowed | Excess Treatment |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Foreign tax < Australian tax | 30% | 20% | Full credit (20%) | No excess |
| Foreign tax = Australian tax | 30% | 30% | Full credit (30%) | No excess |
| Foreign tax > Australian tax | 30% | 35% | Limited to 30% | 5% excess **lost permanently** |
| MIT income (treaty) | 15% | 25% | Limited to 15% | 10% excess lost |

### Key Considerations

- **No separate baskets**: Unlike the US system, Australia uses a single basket -- high-tax and low-tax foreign income are aggregated, which may allow some blending benefit.
- **Timing**: FITO is claimed in the year the foreign tax is paid (or the income is included), with no ability to carry forward timing mismatches.
- **Indirect/underlying credits**: For dividends from foreign subsidiaries (>=10% holding), the old underlying tax credit system (former ITAA 1936 §160AFC) was repealed. Instead, non-portfolio dividends from foreign companies are generally **exempt** from Australian tax under ITAA 1997 §768-5 (non-assessable non-exempt income), eliminating the need for credits.

## 6. CFC Rules

> **Legal Basis**: ITAA 1936 Part X (Sections 317-459A) -- Controlled Foreign Companies

### Overview

Australia has a **comprehensive CFC regime** under Part X of the ITAA 1936. The regime attributes the passive/tainted income of CFCs to their Australian controlling shareholders, preventing indefinite deferral of tax on passive income parked offshore.

### CFC Definition and Thresholds

| Parameter | Detail | Legal Reference |
|---|---|---|
| **CFC definition** | A foreign company where Australian entities hold a **controlling interest** | ITAA 1936 §340 |
| **Controlling interest** | >=50% of voting power, >=50% of value of issued shares, or >=50% of rights to distributions | ITAA 1936 §340(a)-(c) |
| **Attribution percentage** | Proportional to the Australian entity's interest in the CFC | ITAA 1936 §362 |
| **Attributable taxpayer** | Australian entity with >=10% associate-inclusive control interest (or part of a group holding >=50%) | ITAA 1936 §361 |

### Attribution of CFC Income

| Income Category | Description | Legal Reference |
|---|---|---|
| **Passive income** | Interest, dividends, royalties, rent, gains from disposal of passive assets | ITAA 1936 §446 |
| **Tainted sales income** | Income from sale of goods purchased from or sold to associates, where goods not substantially changed | ITAA 1936 §447 |
| **Tainted services income** | Income from services provided to or on behalf of associates | ITAA 1936 §448 |
| **Designated concession income** | Income benefiting from a preferential tax regime in listed countries (formerly "comparable tax" countries) | ITAA 1936 §§431-434 |

### Active Income Exemption

| Parameter | Detail | Legal Reference |
|---|---|---|
| **Threshold** | If >=**95%** of CFC's gross turnover is **active income**, no attribution occurs | ITAA 1936 §457 |
| **Active income** | Income that is NOT passive, tainted sales, or tainted services income | ITAA 1936 §457 |
| **Effect** | CFC passes the active income test: **zero attribution** to Australian parent | |
| **Practical impact** | Operating subsidiaries with genuine business activities are typically exempt from attribution | |

### CFC Attribution Summary

| Scenario | Active Income % | Attribution | Effective Treatment |
|---|---|---|---|
| Operating subsidiary (manufacturing, services) | >=95% active | **No attribution** | Deferral until dividend |
| Holding company (passive investment income) | <95% active | **Attribution of tainted income** | Taxed in Australia when earned |
| Finance subsidiary (intercompany lending) | <95% active (interest is passive) | **Attribution** | No deferral benefit |
| Mixed operations | Between 0-95% active | **Attribution of tainted portion** | Partial attribution |

### Key Exemptions and Concessions

| Provision | Detail | Legal Reference |
|---|---|---|
| **Active income exemption** | >=95% active income: no attribution | ITAA 1936 §457 |
| **De minimis for tainted income** | If tainted income < lesser of AUD 250,000 or 5% of gross turnover, exempt | ITAA 1936 §457(2) |
| **Non-portfolio dividend exemption** | Dividends from >=10% held foreign companies are NANE (non-assessable non-exempt) | ITAA 1997 §768-5 |
| **CFC loss carry-forward** | Attributable losses can be carried forward and offset against future attributable income of the same CFC | ITAA 1936 §431 |

## 7. Tax Treaty Network

> **Legal Basis**: International Tax Agreements Act 1953 (ITAA 1953); Multilateral Convention to Implement Tax Treaty Related Measures (MLI)

### Overview

Australia has one of the most extensive tax treaty networks in the Asia-Pacific, with **45+ comprehensive Double Taxation Agreements (DTAs)** in force. Australia is also a signatory to the OECD **Multilateral Instrument (MLI)**, which modifies the application of many existing treaties.

### Treaty Network Summary

| Parameter | Detail |
|---|---|
| Total comprehensive DTAs in force | **45+** |
| Key treaty partners | US, UK, Japan, China, India, Singapore, NZ, Germany, Canada, South Korea, France, Indonesia, Malaysia, Thailand, Vietnam |
| MLI status | **Signed and ratified** (deposited 2018; entered into force January 1, 2019) |
| Principal Purpose Test (PPT) | **Yes** -- applies to all MLI-covered treaties |
| Limitation on Benefits (LOB) | **No** -- Australia does not apply LOB clauses; relies on PPT via MLI |
| Exchange of Information agreements | **30+ Tax Information Exchange Agreements (TIEAs)** in addition to DTAs |
| Common Reporting Standard (CRS) | Implemented -- automatic exchange of financial account information |

## 8. PE Risk

> **Legal Basis**: ITAA 1936 §6(1) (Permanent establishment definition); ITAA 1997 Division 815 (Transfer pricing); Tax Laws Amendment (Combating Multinational Tax Avoidance) Act 2015 (DPT)

### Domestic PE Definition

Under **ITAA 1936 §6(1)**, a "permanent establishment" in relation to a person means a place at or through which the person carries on any business and includes:

| PE Type | Description | Legal Reference |
|---|---|---|
| **Fixed place of business** | Place of management, branch, office, factory, workshop, mine, oil/gas well, quarry, or other place of extraction of natural resources | ITAA 1936 §6(1)(a) |
| **Construction site** | Building site or construction/installation project (domestic law: no minimum duration) | ITAA 1936 §6(1)(c) |
| **Agricultural/pastoral/forestry** | Place where agricultural, pastoral, or forestry operations are carried on | ITAA 1936 §6(1)(d) |
| **Agent PE** | Place where a person is acting on behalf of another person and has authority to conclude contracts on their behalf | ITAA 1936 §6(1)(e) |
| **Substantial equipment** | Place where substantial equipment is installed for >= 6 months | ITAA 1936 §6(1)(f) |

### Treaty PE Rules (OECD Model Art. 5 Basis)

Most of Australia's DTAs follow the **OECD Model Art. 5** PE definition, which generally provides a higher threshold than domestic law:

| PE Category | Typical Treaty Threshold | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Construction PE** | **12 months** (most treaties) | Site or project must exceed 12 months to constitute PE |
| **Service PE** | **183 days** in any 12-month period (in some treaties) | E.g., Australia-India DTA Art. 5(3)(b): services by employees present for 183+ days |
| **Dependent agent PE** | Agent habitually concluding contracts on behalf of the enterprise | OECD Art. 5(5); modified by MLI Art. 12 |
| **Independent agent PE** | Independent agent acting in ordinary course of business is NOT a PE | OECD Art. 5(6); but lost if acting exclusively or almost exclusively for one principal |
| **Digital PE** | **Not included** in current Australian treaties | No digital PE provisions enacted |
| **Preparatory/auxiliary exemption** | Activities of preparatory or auxiliary character (storage, display, purchasing, information gathering) | OECD Art. 5(4); narrowed by MLI Art. 13 (anti-fragmentation rule) |

### Service PE Variations

| Treaty Partner | Service PE Provision |
|---|---|
| **India** | 183 days in any 12-month period |
| **China** | 183 days in any 12-month period |
| **Indonesia** | 120 days in any 12-month period |
| **Most OECD partners** | No specific service PE (general PE rules apply) |

### Diverted Profits Tax (DPT)

The **Diverted Profits Tax** is a specific anti-avoidance measure targeting multinational enterprises that artificially avoid having an Australian PE or divert profits from Australia:

| Parameter | Detail | Legal Reference |
|---|---|---|
| **DPT rate** | **40%** (penalty rate -- higher than the 30% CIT rate) | ITAA 1936 Division 177H |
| **Applicability** | Significant Global Entities (SGEs) with global income >= AUD 1 billion | ITAA 1936 §177H(1) |
| **Target arrangements** | Schemes where: (1) insufficient economic substance in a low-tax jurisdiction, or (2) artificial avoidance of PE in Australia | ITAA 1936 §177H(3)-(5) |
| **Assessment** | Commissioner issues DPT assessment; taxpayer must pay first, then can dispute | ITAA 1936 §177H(8) |
| **Interaction with Part IVA** | DPT operates alongside (not instead of) the general anti-avoidance rule (Part IVA) | |

### Significant Economic Presence Test

| Parameter | Status |
|---|---|
| **Proposed** | Considered in Treasury discussion papers and OECD Pillar One discussions |
| **Enacted** | **No** -- not currently enacted in Australian law |
| **Digital services tax** | Australia has **not** enacted a unilateral digital services tax, preferring the OECD multilateral approach |

### PE Risk Mitigation Strategies

| Strategy | Description |
|---|---|
| **Commissionaire arrangements** | Use of commissionaires who do not conclude contracts in the enterprise's name -- effectiveness reduced by MLI Art. 12 |
| **Independent agent** | Using genuinely independent agents (not acting exclusively for one principal) |
| **Contract management** | Ensuring contracts are negotiated and concluded outside Australia |
| **Activity review** | Ensuring Australian activities are genuinely preparatory/auxiliary (narrowed by MLI anti-fragmentation rule) |
| **Treaty planning** | Structuring operations under favorable treaty PE definitions |
