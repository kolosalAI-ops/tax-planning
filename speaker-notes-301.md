## Slide 5: VAT, GST & Sales Tax Comparison

As the bar chart comparing indirect tax rates illustrates, indirect taxes hit cash flow before they hit the income statement, and in a cross-border supply chain, irrecoverable VAT or GST is a real cost. Mexico's IVA at sixteen percent standard with an eight percent border zone rate creates an immediate planning opportunity for companies locating near the US border. Indonesia's PPN increase to twelve percent from January 2025 — up from eleven — is a meaningful change for companies with significant Indonesian procurement. Canada's split system — five percent federal GST versus thirteen to fifteen percent harmonised HST depending on province — means the same transaction can carry different indirect tax burdens depending on which province the supply occurs in. And then there is Brazil — the most complex indirect tax system in our group, with PIS/COFINS at nine point two five percent non-cumulative, layered with state ICMS at seven to twenty-five percent and municipal ISS at two to five percent. The landmark reform under EC 132/2023 will replace all five taxes with CBS and IBS by 2033, which we cover in detail on the next slide.

## Slide 7: Brazil Tax Reform — CBS/IBS Timeline 2026–2033

The timeline visualization on this slide deserves significant attention because Brazil's indirect tax reform under EC 132/2023 is the most significant tax legislative change in any of our seven jurisdictions this decade. The reform replaces five separate indirect taxes — PIS, COFINS, IPI, ICMS, and ISS — with two new value-added taxes: CBS at the federal level and IBS at the state and municipal level. The timeline is a seven-year phased transition. In 2026, CBS pilots at zero point nine percent and IBS at zero point one percent while all existing taxes remain in full — this is a parallel-run year for systems and compliance testing. In 2027, PIS and COFINS are fully replaced by CBS, and IPI drops to zero except for Manaus Free Zone products. From 2029 through 2032, ICMS and ISS phase out gradually at ten percent per year while IBS rates increase proportionally. By 2033, full transition: ICMS and ISS are extinguished and CBS plus IBS become the sole indirect taxes. Three design features to note: first, the destination principle means tax is collected where goods and services are consumed, not where produced — this eliminates the inter-state tax wars that have plagued Brazil's ICMS system for decades. Second, the combined CBS plus IBS rate is estimated at roughly twenty-six point five percent, which would be one of the world's highest VAT rates. Third, a cashback mechanism will refund tax on basic goods to lower-income families through the Bolsa Família programme. For multinationals, the action item is to model the transition's impact on pricing, cash flow timing, and input credit recovery during the phase-in period. Systems that currently handle five different tax calculations will need to handle seven during transition and two afterward.

## Slide 9: Territorial vs. Worldwide Tax Systems

Before choosing where to set up subsidiaries, first understand what your parent country will do with the profits earned abroad — this is the single most important structural question. A territorial system taxes only domestic income; foreign income is generally exempt. Singapore is the only pure territorial system in our group — foreign-sourced income is taxed only when remitted to Singapore, and even then, broad exemptions under ITA section 13(8) mean qualified foreign dividends, branch profits, and service income come in tax-free. A worldwide system taxes all income regardless of where earned, with foreign taxes potentially credited against domestic liability. The United States, Canada, Australia, Brazil, Mexico, and Indonesia all operate worldwide systems — but each layers CFC rules on top that determine when and how they reach into your foreign subsidiaries. The practical consequence is profound: Singapore as parent gives you maximum flexibility — no CFC rules, no tax on foreign dividends or profits, zero capital gains tax on disposal. Brazil as parent is the most restrictive — universal CFC taxation means all foreign profits are taxed annually at thirty-four percent with zero deferral. The US falls in between — complex, with GILTI imposing a ten point five percent minimum on all CFC earnings, but at least offering a high-tax exclusion and FTC relief. The parent country's system determines your CFC exposure, your FTC complexity, and whether an intermediate holding company adds or destroys value.

## Slide 10: CFC Rules — What Are They & Why Do They Matter?

CFC stands for Controlled Foreign Company — a foreign subsidiary where the parent country's residents own a controlling stake. CFC rules force the parent to include certain foreign subsidiary income in its own tax return, even if no dividends are paid. The goal is preventing profit deferral by parking income in low-tax jurisdictions indefinitely. What varies across our seven countries is substantial. First, the ownership threshold: when are you considered controlling? Second, what income gets caught: all income, only passive income, or income below a rate threshold? Third, whether active business income is exempted. Fourth, whether foreign tax credits are available to offset the double taxation. The severity ranking shown in the bar chart on this slide is critical for structuring decisions. Brazil sits at the top as most restrictive — universal CFC means all foreign profits are taxed annually with no active income exemption under Lei 12.973 Articles 76 through 92. The United States is next with GILTI plus Subpart F, capturing all CFC income subject to a ten point five percent minimum under IRC section 951A. Canada's FAPI regime taxes passive income but exempts active business income earned in treaty countries. Australia's Part X CFC rules tax passive income but offer a ninety-five percent active income safe harbour. Mexico's REFIPRE only targets passive income in jurisdictions below twenty-two point five percent effective rate. Indonesia's deemed dividend rule has limited enforcement in practice. And Singapore has no CFC rules at all — this is why it remains the most popular parent jurisdiction in Southeast Asia.

## Slide 11: CFC Thresholds & Planning Points

Let us get specific on the triggers. The United States uses a ten percent individual plus fifty percent aggregate ownership test — and once you are a CFC, GILTI catches everything. The high-tax exclusion is your primary planning lever: if the CFC pays an effective rate of at least eighteen point nine percent — that is ninety percent of the twenty-one percent US rate — you can exclude the income from GILTI. Indonesia at twenty-two percent, Canada at twenty-three to twenty-seven percent, and Australia at twenty-five to thirty percent naturally qualify for this exclusion. Singapore at seventeen percent does not qualify, meaning GILTI applies at ten point five percent on Singapore CFC earnings. Canada's FAPI rules have a valuable feature: active business income earned in treaty countries qualifies for exempt surplus treatment, meaning dividends flow to the Canadian parent tax-free under ITA section 113. For Australia, the key planning point is keeping each CFC at ninety-five percent or more active income to stay outside the Part X CFC net. For Brazil, no planning avoids the CFC inclusion — the only strategy is maximising foreign tax credits to offset the thirty-four percent domestic rate, and critically, Brazil offers no FTC carry-forward — it is use-it-or-lose-it in the year incurred. For the US and Canada, FTC carry-forwards of ten years provide a cushion against timing mismatches.

## Slide 12: US GILTI, BEAT & FDII — The Trifecta

This is one of the most important slides in the deck because the calculation table shown here illustrates the actual arithmetic of the US international tax trifecta. Let us walk through the GILTI calculation first. Our example: a Singapore CFC with fifty million dollars of tested income and one hundred million in tangible assets — QBAI. Step one: net deemed tangible income return is ten percent of QBAI, so ten million. Step two: net GILTI inclusion is fifty million minus ten million, equals forty million. Step three: the section 250 deduction is fifty percent — but note the phase-down, this drops to thirty-seven point five percent after 2025 — giving us twenty million of taxable GILTI. Step four: tax at twenty-one percent produces four point two million, for an effective rate on the full fifty million of eight point four percent. That is the current GILTI cost of a Singapore CFC. After 2025, the rate rises from ten point five percent effective to thirteen point one two five percent. On BEAT: this is the Base Erosion and Anti-Abuse Tax, a ten percent minimum on modified taxable income that adds back deductible payments to foreign affiliates. The threshold is five hundred million in three-year average gross receipts plus base erosion payments exceeding three percent of total deductions. It catches intercompany royalties, management fees, and interest to foreign affiliates — but not cost of goods sold and not services eligible for the Services Cost Method exception. On FDII: the Foreign-Derived Intangible Income deduction gives a thirty-seven point five percent deduction on income from serving foreign markets, producing an effective thirteen point one two five percent rate — but this also phases down after 2025. The strategic tension is clear: GILTI incentivises placing IP offshore to reduce the CFC inclusion, while FDII incentivises keeping IP onshore to access the lower export rate. Post-2025, the gap between them narrows significantly.

## Slide 13: Brazil Universal CFC & JCP Optimization

Brazil's CFC regime is uniquely aggressive — as the JCP comparison table on this slide demonstrates, all foreign profits of controlled entities are taxed in Brazil annually at thirty-four percent, regardless of whether they are distributed, regardless of whether the income is active or passive, and regardless of the foreign tax rate. This is Lei 12.973 Articles 76 through 92. There is no active income exemption — unlike every other country in our analysis. Foreign tax credits are available but only with reciprocity or a treaty, and crucially, there is no carry-forward. Profits and losses of different CFCs may be offset, but only within the same fiscal year. For Brazilian parents, the only real optimisation levers are maximising FTC utilisation and using Juros sobre Capital Próprio for inbound flows. Let us walk through the JCP worked example on the slide. Take a company with a one hundred million reais equity base. Under the dividend route: pre-tax profit of one hundred million, CIT at thirty-four percent takes thirty-four million, dividends are exempt from withholding, and the parent receives sixty-six million with a thirty-four percent effective rate. Under the JCP route: the company deducts twelve million of JCP calculated at the TJLP rate of roughly twelve percent times the equity base. Taxable income drops to eighty-eight million, CIT at thirty-four percent is twenty-nine point nine million, and there is a fifteen percent withholding tax on the JCP payment of one point eight million. Total tax is thirty-one point seven million at a thirty-one point seven percent effective rate, and the parent receives sixty-eight point three million. JCP saves two point three percentage points on every cycle. On a one billion reais equity base, that is twenty-three million reais per year in tax savings. Always use JCP when available — it is limited to TJLP times equity under Lei 9.249 Article 9.

## Slide 14: How to Pick a Holding Company Jurisdiction

Start with your parent country's CFC regime — this is the number one factor, not the holding company's local tax rate. If you are a Singapore parent, use Singapore itself as the holding company. Why? No CFC rules, zero dividend withholding, zero capital gains tax, and over ninety tax treaties. There is simply no benefit to an intermediate holding company when the parent already has the ideal profile. For a Canadian parent, hold subsidiaries in treaty countries so that active business income dividends qualify for exempt surplus treatment under ITA section 113 — dividends from treaty country subsidiaries with active business income flow to Canada tax-free. For an Australian parent, ensure each foreign subsidiary has ninety-five percent or more active income to stay outside the Part X CFC net — operating subsidiaries with genuine substance work well, but avoid IP-heavy or passive-income CFCs. For a US parent, structure for the GILTI High-Tax Exclusion by pushing CFC effective rates above eighteen point nine percent, and consider FDII for US-based export income. For a Mexico parent, ensure foreign subsidiaries have more than seventy-five percent active income and pay more than twenty-two point five percent local rate to avoid REFIPRE. For a Brazil parent, no holding structure avoids universal CFC — focus entirely on FTC maximisation and JCP optimisation. The post-Pillar 2 overlay is important: for in-scope MNEs above seven hundred fifty million euros, the holding jurisdiction must deliver at least fifteen percent ETR or you are paying top-up tax somewhere.

## Slide 15: Branch vs. Subsidiary — When to Use Which

The branch versus subsidiary decision is your first entity-level choice in a new market, and getting it wrong is expensive to unwind. Use a branch when you are expecting initial losses, because branch losses can offset headquarters profits in the US, Canada, and most worldwide-system countries — that is immediate cash value. Use a branch for temporary projects, particularly construction or consulting engagements that may stay below PE thresholds. Brazil and Australia have zero percent branch profits tax, so there is no extra cost on remittance — making branches viable for longer-term operations in those markets. Singapore's branch profits tax is also zero. Use a subsidiary when the operation is profitable from the start, because subsidiaries access local incentives that branches cannot: Indonesia's Tax Holiday offering zero percent CIT for up to twenty years, Canada's Small Business Deduction at nine percent, Singapore's Pioneer Certificate, Australia's Base Rate Entity at twenty-five percent. Use a subsidiary for long-term operations where asset protection, local market credibility, and the ability to reinvest without triggering branch profits tax matter. But watch the branch tax trap: US branch profits tax is thirty percent and Canada's is twenty-five percent, so combined branch rates in those jurisdictions hit forty-four point seven percent for the US and thirty-six point three percent for Canada — far worse than the subsidiary-plus-treaty-dividend route. Dividend repatriation via treaty typically delivers much lower effective rates than branch profits tax.

## Slide 17: Withholding Taxes & Treaty Networks

The WHT rates table on this slide shows the default versus best treaty rate on dividends for each jurisdiction, and the spreads are dramatic. Singapore's default is zero — the one-tier imputation system under ITA section 44 means there is never withholding on dividends, regardless of treaty status. Brazil's default on dividends is also zero, though JCP carries a fifteen percent withholding. Mexico's default is ten percent but the best treaty rate reaches zero on pre-2014 CUFIN distributions. Indonesia's default is twenty percent, reducible to ten percent under the best treaties. Canada's default is twenty-five percent, dropping to five percent under treaties with substantial shareholding requirements. The United States has the highest default at thirty percent, reducible to five percent under its best treaties. Australia is nuanced: thirty percent on unfranked dividends but zero on franked dividends regardless of treaty. On the right side, note the key corridors. The US-Canada corridor is exceptionally efficient: five percent dividends, zero interest, zero to ten percent royalties. The Singapore-Indonesia corridor runs at ten percent across all three categories. And critically, there is no comprehensive tax treaty between the United States and Singapore — only a limited shipping and aircraft agreement. This means US-source dividends to a Singapore parent face the full thirty percent statutory withholding, making the US-Singapore corridor one of the most expensive in our analysis and creating strong incentive to route through an intermediary jurisdiction with treaty access to both.

## Slide 19: Foreign Tax Credit Mechanics

This slide maps the FTC architecture across all seven jurisdictions, and the differences matter enormously for cash tax outcomes. The US system is the most complex, operating with four separate baskets under IRC section 904(d). The general basket covers active business income with a ten-year carry-forward and one-year carry-back. The passive basket covers dividends, interest, royalties, rents, and capital gains — same carry-forward and carry-back periods. The GILTI basket is unique and punitive: no carry-forward, no carry-back, single-year use only, and limited to eighty percent of foreign taxes under section 960(d). The foreign branch basket covers income attributable to foreign branches, again with ten-year carry-forward. The critical constraint is the cross-basket limitation: excess credits in one basket cannot offset tax in another. If you have excess credits in the general basket from a high-tax jurisdiction like Brazil, those credits do nothing for your GILTI shortfall or your passive income exposure. This creates permanent double taxation risk if your income streams are not balanced across baskets. Canada uses a per-country system with business versus non-business income splitting: business FTCs carry forward ten years with three-year carry-back, but non-business FTCs have no carry-forward at all. Australia pools everything into a single FITO basket with no carry-forward. Singapore offers per-country with an optional pooling election under section 50A, but also no carry-forward. Indonesia, Mexico, and Brazil all operate per-country with no carry-forward. The planning implication is clear: in jurisdictions without carry-forward — which is most of our seven — excess FTCs are permanently lost. You must plan intercompany pricing and dividend timing to match FTC capacity each year.

## Slide 20: FTC Basket Calculation — Multi-Jurisdiction Case

This is a worked example — as the calculation table on the slide details — that shows exactly how the FTC basket mechanics play out for a US parent with three subsidiaries — Singapore, Indonesia, and Australia — all in the general basket. Singapore subsidiary: ten million in pre-tax income, CIT paid at seventeen percent is one point seven million, zero WHT on dividends, total foreign tax one point seven million. US tax at twenty-one percent on ten million is two point one million. FTC allowed is capped at the lesser of foreign tax or US tax — so one point seven million. Net US tax: four hundred thousand. Singapore under-credits because seventeen percent is less than the twenty-one percent US rate. Indonesia subsidiary: eight million pre-tax, CIT at twenty-two percent is one point seven six million, WHT at ten percent is six hundred twenty thousand, total foreign tax two point three eight million. US tax at twenty-one percent on eight million is one point six eight million. FTC capped at one point six eight million — seven hundred thousand in excess credits. Australia subsidiary: six million pre-tax, CIT at thirty percent is one point eight million, zero WHT on franked dividends, total foreign tax one point eight million. US tax at twenty-one percent on six million is one point two six million. FTC capped at one point two six million — five hundred forty thousand in excess credits. Now, because all three are in the same general basket, the excess credits from Indonesia and Australia — totalling one point two four million — can cross-credit against the Singapore shortfall of four hundred thousand. Net result: US pays only four hundred thousand on twenty-four million of foreign income — one point seven percent incremental, for a combined ETR of twenty-six point two percent. This cross-crediting within baskets is why basket classification is so important.

## Slide 21: Australia Franking Credit System Deep-Dive

The Australian franking credit system, as the side-by-side comparison visual on this slide highlights, is unique among our seven jurisdictions and can create a ten-point-five-percentage-point ETR swing depending on whether dividends are franked or unfranked. Scenario A — fully franked dividend: the Australian subsidiary earns ten million pre-tax, pays three million in CIT at thirty percent, distributes seven million in after-tax profit with three million in franking credits attached. Withholding tax on franked dividends: zero under Division 202 of ITAA 1997. Cash received by the Singapore parent: seven million. Total ETR: thirty percent. Singapore FSIE exemption applies because Australia's headline rate exceeds fifteen percent. Scenario B — unfranked dividend: same pre-tax income, same CIT, but the dividend carries no franking credit — this happens when the dividend is paid from exempt income, or the franking account is depleted. WHT on unfranked dividends under the Singapore-Australia treaty: fifteen percent of seven million is one point zero five million. Cash received: five point nine five million. Total ETR: forty point five percent — that is ten and a half percentage points worse. The planning imperative is clear: always maintain sufficient franking account balance before declaring dividends, and time dividend declarations around tax payments to maximise franking availability. Additionally, Division 802 on conduit foreign income allows foreign-sourced income flowing through an Australian company to be distributed as unfranked plus conduit foreign income at zero percent WHT to non-residents — a powerful tool for Australian holding structures receiving and redistributing foreign income.

## Slide 22: Singapore FSIE & Pillar 2 DTT Interaction

Singapore's Foreign-Sourced Income Exemption under ITA section 13(8) is one of its most valuable features, and the worked example table on this slide walks through the three-condition test, which is straightforward. Condition one: the income has been subject to tax in the source jurisdiction with a headline rate of at least fifteen percent. Condition two: the highest CIT rate of the source country is at least fifteen percent at the time the income is received in Singapore. Condition three: the company is tax-resident in Singapore and demonstrates economic substance. All six peer countries in our analysis qualify — US at twenty-one percent, Canada at fifteen percent federal plus provincial, Indonesia at twenty-two percent, Brazil at thirty-four percent, Mexico at thirty percent, and Australia at thirty percent. This means Singapore-sourced income from any of these jurisdictions is fully exempt when remitted. Now, the Pillar 2 overlay: Singapore enacted its Domestic Minimum Top-up Tax effective January 2025 for MNE groups with more than seven hundred fifty million euros in consolidated revenue. It is a Qualified DMTT, meaning the top-up tax stays in Singapore rather than being ceded to the parent country via the Income Inclusion Rule. The SBIE — Substance-Based Income Exclusion — deducts five percent of eligible payroll costs plus five percent of tangible asset carrying value from the GloBE base, reducing the top-up amount. The worked example on the slide shows a pioneer company paying five percent CIT: pre-Pillar 2, total tax on fifty million is two point five million at five percent. Post-Pillar 2, the DTT top-up of five million brings total tax to seven point five million at fifteen percent. For in-scope MNEs, rate-based incentives like Pioneer, DEI, and GTP effectively floor at fifteen percent. Groups under seven hundred fifty million euros are completely unaffected.

## Slide 24: Interest Deductibility & Thin Capitalisation

The convergence toward EBITDA-based limits is now nearly universal in our seven jurisdictions. The United States limits net business interest expense to thirty percent of adjusted taxable income under IRC section 163(j), with a twenty-nine million dollar de minimis threshold and indefinite carry-forward of disallowed interest. Canada's EIFEL rules under ITA section 18.2, effective June 2024, impose the same thirty percent of tax-EBITDA cap, but with a critical difference: they apply to interest paid to both related and arm's length lenders, unlike many OECD peers. The de minimis is two hundred fifty thousand Canadian dollars of net interest, and excess capacity carries forward twenty years. Mexico uses a hybrid: thirty percent of EBITDA plus a three-to-one debt-to-equity ratio, with disallowed interest carried forward ten years. Australia has replaced its old safe harbour DER test with a thirty percent of tax-EBITDA rule from July 2024, with a two million Australian dollar de minimis and fifteen-year carry-forward. Now for the outliers: Indonesia still uses a generous four-to-one debt-to-equity ratio under PMK 169/2015, with excess interest permanently non-deductible — no carry-forward. Brazil applies a two-to-one DER for related parties and a punitive zero point three-to-one ratio for lending from tax haven jurisdictions under Lei 12.249 Articles 24 through 26. And Singapore has no fixed limit at all — interest must simply be arm's length under ITA section 34D, making it the most flexible regime for intercompany financing. The design principle is clear: structure your intercompany debt before operations begin, because exceeding these limits creates permanent disallowance in most jurisdictions.

## Slide 26: Canada EIFEL & SBD Phase-Out Mechanics

Canada's EIFEL rules deserve dedicated attention because they are the newest major interest limitation in our seven jurisdictions, effective June 2024. The cap is thirty percent of tax-EBITDA, but unlike many peers, EIFEL applies to interest paid to both related and arm's length lenders — this is broader than the OECD recommendation and catches acquisition debt from third-party banks, not just intercompany loans. At fifteen million in capital, SBD is fully eliminated. ## Slide 28: Where to Place Your R&D Hub

The choice of R&D location should now be driven by one overriding factor for in-scope MNEs: whether the incentive is a Qualified Refundable Tax Credit or a rate reduction. QRTCs are excluded from the GloBE ETR calculation, meaning they deliver their full stated benefit regardless of Pillar 2. Rate reductions push the local ETR below fifteen percent and trigger top-up tax. Singapore's EIS at four hundred percent enhanced deduction on qualifying R&D under ITA section 14C delivers excellent value — one million spend generates two hundred seventy-two thousand in tax savings — but it is a rate reduction. For in-scope MNEs, the EIS effect is capped at what the fifteen percent floor allows. Indonesia's three hundred percent super deduction similarly delivers four hundred forty thousand per million at twenty-two percent CIT, but it too is a rate reduction. Canada's SR&ED programme is the standout: thirty-five percent refundable ITC on the first three million for CCPCs under ITA section 127.44. Refundable means cash back even if there is no tax liability, and it is a QRTC — fully safe under Pillar 2. Australia's forty-three point five percent refundable offset for small companies is also QRTC-safe. The US R&D credit at twenty percent under IRC section 41 qualifies as QRTC as well, though since 2022, R&D expenditure must be amortised over five years domestic or fifteen years foreign under section 174 — a significant cash flow change. Brazil's Lei do Bem at sixty to eighty percent extra deduction and Mexico's thirty percent incremental EFIDT credit round out the options. The bottom line: for groups above seven hundred fifty million euros, shift R&D from rate-reduction jurisdictions to QRTC jurisdictions.

## Slide 31: Global Minimum Tax — What Changes in 2025

The Pillar 2 GloBE rules impose a fifteen percent minimum ETR for MNE groups with more than seven hundred fifty million euros in consolidated revenue. If a group's ETR in any jurisdiction falls below fifteen percent, the parent country collects a top-up tax. Let me walk through the adoption status table and SBIE calculation shown on this slide: Singapore has enacted its Qualified DMTT from January 2025. Canada has enacted both DMTT and UTPR. ## Slide 35: Transfer Pricing Documentation Requirements

All seven jurisdictions require arm's length pricing on intercompany transactions — no exceptions. The documentation framework follows the OECD three-tier model: Master File providing a group-wide overview, Local File with detailed entity-level analysis, and Country-by-Country Report for groups above the revenue threshold. The US is the outlier — it uses IRC section 482 documentation instead of a formal Local File but the substance is similar. The most significant development here is Brazil's shift to OECD arm's length standard under Lei 14.596/2023, replacing the old fixed-margin methods — PRL, PIC, and CPL — with CUP, TNMM, and profit split. This is the most significant transfer pricing change in Brazilian history and companies must rebuild their TP documentation from scratch. CbCR thresholds vary: US at eight hundred fifty million, Canada at C seven hundred fifty million, Indonesia at IDR eleven trillion, Brazil at R two point two six billion, Mexico at MXN twelve billion, Australia at A one billion, Singapore at S one point one two five billion. Penalty regimes also vary dramatically: the US imposes twenty to forty percent penalties, while Australia can reach twenty-five to fifty percent and Mexico fifty to seventy-five percent. Australia's Diverted Profits Tax adds a forty percent penalty rate — ten percentage points above standard CIT — for MNEs with more than one billion in global revenue and twenty-five million in Australian revenue that artificially shift profits offshore. This sits on top of the Part IVA GAAR and is an effective deterrent.

## Slide 36: Permanent Establishment — When Does a Tax Obligation Arise?

A PE is a sufficient physical or commercial presence that triggers local tax obligations on profits attributable to that presence. Accidentally creating a PE means filing local tax returns and paying local CIT without advance planning or incentives — this is one of the costliest unforced errors in cross-border tax. Construction PE thresholds split into two groups: Indonesia, Brazil, and Mexico use six months — a shorter threshold creating higher risk. The US, Canada, Australia, and Singapore use twelve months — the standard OECD threshold. Service PE thresholds are where Indonesia stands out: just sixty days of service creates a PE — extremely short, meaning even brief consulting engagements trigger full PE exposure. Brazil, Mexico, Canada, Australia, and Singapore all use one hundred eighty-three days. The US has no specific service PE rule, relying instead on the fixed place of business test. Agent PE is universal: a dependent agent who habitually concludes contracts can create a PE in all seven jurisdictions. Indonesia's PE definition is the broadest with eighteen categories. Watch for digital PE developments — no formal digital PE exists in any of our seven jurisdictions, but Indonesia applies VAT on digital services and Australia uses its Multinational Anti-Avoidance Law to address digital presence.

## Slide 37: Construction PE Threshold Arbitrage

The PE threshold comparison table shown on this slide covers all seven jurisdictions — construction, service, agent, and digital — and highlights the high-risk scenarios with mitigation strategies. Indonesia's sixty-day service PE is the most dangerous trap: two months of on-site consulting with the same personnel creates a full PE, triggering Indonesian tax filing obligations and twenty-two percent CIT on attributable profits. Mitigation: structure as short rotations with different personnel, carefully tracking individual days. The six-month construction PE in Indonesia, Brazil, and Mexico requires phasing projects to stay under threshold or using local subcontractors for the execution phase while keeping design and project management offshore. Agent PE is increasingly scrutinised post-BEPS: local sales representatives who negotiate and close deals create PE even without a physical office. Commissionnaire structures — where the agent acts in their own name but for the principal's account — can reduce agent PE risk, but post-BEPS Article 12 narrowed this significantly. Contract structuring is a viable mitigation: split large projects into sub-threshold phases with genuine commercial rationale, not artificial splitting that would be caught by substance-over-form doctrines. Personnel rotation requires meticulous tracking — the sixty-day Indonesian threshold means you need different team members for sequential visits, with proper documentation of the rotation rationale.

## Slide 38: GAAR, Anti-Treaty Shopping & Substance

Every jurisdiction has a General Anti-Avoidance Rule, and each uses a slightly different test. The US codified its Economic Substance Doctrine under IRC section 7701(o). Canada's ITA section 245 applies a misuse-and-abuse test with the CRA actively challenging aggressive structures. Australia's Part IVA uses a dominant purpose test — if the dominant purpose of an arrangement is to obtain a tax benefit, the arrangement can be voided. Indonesia uses substance over form under Pasal 18(3). Brazil requires a business purpose under the general rules. Mexico introduced a business reason test in 2020 under CFF Article 5-A. Singapore's ITA section 33 allows tax authorities to disregard arrangements designed to avoid tax. On the treaty side, two anti-abuse mechanisms dominate. Limitation on Benefits requires genuine resident status with substantial activities — used in all US treaties, as well as Singapore, Canada, Mexico, and Indonesia treaties. Principal Purpose Test denies treaty benefits if the main purpose was obtaining the benefit — used in Canada, Australia, Singapore, and Mexico via the MLI. The US has not signed the MLI, so there is no PPT in US treaties — only LOB. The substance requirement is the thread connecting all of these: every holding company, IP entity, or financing vehicle must have genuine management, decision-making, staff, and office space. Shell entities face GAAR challenge, treaty denial, and Pillar 2 SBIE denial simultaneously.

## Slide 44: Multi-Jurisdiction Loss Utilisation Cycle

As the timeline and comparison table on this slide illustrate, this slide presents a branch-to-subsidiary conversion strategy that maximises the value of startup losses across jurisdictions. The cycle has four phases. Years one and two — the branch phase: operate as a branch of the Singapore, US, or Canadian parent. Years four onward — the subsidiary phase: convert the branch to a locally incorporated subsidiary. Now the entity can access local incentives — Indonesia Tax Holiday, Canada SBD, Australia BRE, Singapore PTE — that were unavailable to a branch. ## Slide 51: Cross-Border Tax Planning Checklist

This checklist is organised into three phases and should be treated as a living document throughout the life of your cross-border structure. Pre-entry items: assess parent country CFC exposure — this is step one before any other decision. Select the holding jurisdiction based on both CFC rules and treaty access. Run the branch versus subsidiary ETR analysis we discussed in slide fifteen. Design capitalisation within thin cap and EIFEL limits — do this before operations begin, not after. Apply for incentives — Tax Holiday applications in Indonesia, Pioneer Status in Singapore, SR&ED registration in Canada, and PTE elections. Establish transfer pricing policy and documentation from day one. Map the treaty network for all planned corridors. Assess Pillar 2 in-scope status if group revenue exceeds seven hundred fifty million euros. Model DMTT and IIR impact on concessionary rates. Check FIRB, CFIUS, BKPM, and ICA screening requirements. Review land and real property ownership restrictions. Operational items: maintain Master File, Local File, and CbCR. Monitor thin cap DER and EBITDA ratios quarterly — not annually. Track EIFEL restricted interest carry-forwards in Canada. Track NOL carry-forwards within expiry limits, especially the five-year window in Indonesia. Optimise R&D location based on QRTC versus rate reduction analysis. File DGT Forms in Indonesia and W-8BEN-E in the US on time — expired forms mean full statutory WHT. Maintain economic substance for all entities. Monitor PE exposure for cross-border services. Review GAAR compliance on intercompany transactions. Manage VAT and GST registration and input credit recovery. Track Pillar 2 GloBE returns. Repatriation and exit items: use JCP, not dividends, for Brazil repatriation. Time dividend declarations to maximise Australian franking credits. Plan share disposal structures through Singapore for zero CGT.
