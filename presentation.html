Corporate Tax Rates & SME Regimes

Headline CIT Rates
  • Singapore: 17% flat — lowest headline rate; effective as low as 4.25% under SUTE/PTE (ITA §43A/43B)
  • United States: 21% flat (IRC §11(b)); CAMT: 15% corporate AMT on adjusted financial statement income for groups with 3-yr avg ≥$1B (IRC §55)
    • CAMT applies to book income after adjustments — affects companies with low ETR from accelerated depreciation, stock comp, or credits
  • Indonesia: 22% standard; 50% reduction on taxable income ≤IDR 50B (Pasal 31E); listed companies get 19% (3pp discount for ≥40% public float)
  • Canada: 15% federal + 8-16% provincial = 23-31% combined (ITA §123(1), §124)
    • SBD phase-out: 9% rate lost when taxable capital exceeds C$10M (fully clawed back at C$15M); also lost when prior-year passive income exceeds C$50K (ITA §125(5.1))
  • Australia: 30% general; 25% base rate entities (<A$50M turnover, ≤80% passive) (ITAA 1997 §23-5)
  • Mexico: 30% flat (LISR Art. 9); ~20% effective in northern border zone (1/3 ISR credit)
  • Brazil: 34% combined — IRPJ 25% + CSLL 9% (Lei 9.249/1995 Art. 3°, Lei 7.689/1988)
SME & Reduced Regimes
  • SG Startup (SUTE): 75% exempt on first S$100K + 50% on next S$100K for first 3 years = effective 4.25%
  • SG PTE: 75% exempt on first S$10K + 50% on next S$190K = effective 8.29% on first S$200K
  • Canada SBD: 9% federal on first C$500K for CCPCs (ITA §125(1)) = ~12.2% combined
    • Phase-out: taxable capital >C$10M or passive income >C$50K
  • Indonesia MSME: 0.5% final tax on gross revenue ≤IDR 4.8B/year (PP 55/2022)
  • Brazil Simples Nacional: 4-33% progressive on gross revenue ≤R$4.8M (LC 123/2006)
    • Lucro Presumido: ~6-11% effective for service/trade companies ≤R$78M revenue
  • Mexico RESICO: 1-2.5% for individuals ≤MXN 3.5M (LISR Art. 113-E); no corporate equivalent
  • Australia BRE: 25% vs 30% for turnover <A$50M (ITAA 1997 §23-12)
Range: A startup paying 0.5% final tax in Indonesia or 4.25% in Singapore vs 34% in Brazil — a 30pp spread before any structuring.

Brazil Tax Reform

Brazil Tax Reform: CBS/IBS Timeline 2026–2033

EC 132/2023 replaces 5 indirect taxes (PIS, COFINS, IPI, ICMS, ISS) with two new value-added taxes: CBS (federal) and IBS (state/municipal).

Reform Timeline
2026
CBS pilot at 0.9%; IBS pilot at 0.1%. Existing taxes remain in full.
2027
PIS/COFINS fully replaced by CBS. IPI reduced to zero except for Manaus Free Zone products.
2029–2032
Gradual ICMS/ISS phase-out: rates reduced 10%/year while IBS rate increases proportionally.
2033
Full transition: ICMS and ISS fully extinguished. CBS + IBS as sole indirect taxes. Destination-based collection.
Key Design Features
  • Destination principle: tax collected where goods/services are consumed, not where produced — eliminates inter-state tax wars
  • Combined CBS + IBS rate: estimated at ~26.5% (one of the world's highest VAT rates)
  • Broad base: fewer exemptions than current system; reduced rates for health, education, public transport, agriculture
  • Cashback mechanism: lower-income families receive refund of tax on basic goods via Bolsa Família
  • Selective tax (IS): new excise on tobacco, alcohol, sugary beverages, mineral extraction, and polluting products
Impact: Compliance simplification from 5 taxes to 2, but the ~26.5% combined rate is steep. MNEs should model the transition's impact on pricing, cash flow timing, and credit recovery during the 7-year phase-in.

CFC Rules

What Are CFC Rules & Why Do They Matter?

What Are CFC Rules?
  • CFC = Controlled Foreign Company — a foreign subsidiary where the parent country's residents own a controlling stake
  • CFC rules force the parent to include certain foreign subsidiary income in its own tax return — even if no dividends are paid
  • The goal: prevent profit deferral by parking income in low-tax jurisdictions indefinitely
What Varies by Country
  • Ownership threshold (when are you "controlling"?)
  • What income gets caught (all, passive only, or below a rate threshold?)
  • Whether active business income is exempted
  • Whether foreign tax credits offset double taxation

CFC Rules

CFC Thresholds & Planning Points

Ownership & Income Triggers
  • US: 10% individual + 50% aggregate — catches all CFC income via GILTI
  • Canada: 10% + 50% combined — only passive + service income caught
  • Australia: 50% control — passive income caught; 95% active income test exempts most operating subs
  • Mexico: 50% control — only passive income in jurisdictions below 22.5% effective rate
  • Indonesia: 50% control — deemed dividend on passive income
  • Brazil: 50% control — all income regardless of character or source country rate
  • Singapore: No threshold — no CFC regime exists
Key Planning Points
  • US GILTI High-Tax Exclusion: if the CFC pays an effective rate ≥18.9% (90% of 21%), GILTI inclusion can be excluded
    • Indonesia (22%), Canada (23-27%), Australia (25-30%) naturally qualify
    • Singapore (17%) does NOT qualify — GILTI applies at 10.5%
  • Canada FAPI: active business income earned in treaty countries qualifies for "exempt surplus" — dividends flow to Canada tax-free
  • Australia Part X: keep each CFC at 95%+ active income to stay outside the CFC net
  • Brazil: no planning avoids CFC inclusion — focus instead on maximizing foreign tax credits to offset the 34% domestic rate
  • FTC carry-forward: US allows 10 years, Canada 10 years, others generally none
Planning priority: For US parents, always model the GILTI HTE election across all CFCs before year-end. For Australia, the 95% active income test is binary — one passive income stream can pull the entire CFC into the net. Structure IP licensing separately from operating entities.

BEAT & FDII
  • BEAT (IRC §59A): 10% minimum tax on "modified taxable income" (adds back deductible payments to foreign affiliates)
    • Threshold: $500M 3-yr avg gross receipts + base erosion payments ≥3% of total deductions
    • Catches: intercompany royalties, management fees, interest to foreign affiliates
    • Does NOT catch: COGS, services eligible for SCM exception
  • FDII (IRC §250): 37.5% deduction on income from serving foreign markets = effective 13.125% rate
    • Applies to: US-based companies earning export income from IP, services, property sold for foreign use
    • Phase-down: deduction drops from 37.5% to 21.875% after 2025 → effective rate rises to ~16.4%
  • Strategic interaction: GILTI incentivizes placing IP offshore (lower inclusion), while FDII incentivizes keeping IP onshore (lower rate on exports). Post-2025, the gap narrows significantly.

Structuring

How to Pick a Holding Company Jurisdiction

  • Start with your parent country's CFC regime — this is the #1 factor, not the holding company's local tax rate
  • Singapore parent → use Singapore itself as holding company
    • No CFC rules, 0% dividend WHT, 0% capital gains tax, 90+ tax treaties
  • Canada parent → hold subsidiaries in treaty countries for "exempt surplus"
    • Active business income dividends from treaty country subs = tax-free under ITA §113
  • Australia parent → ensure each foreign sub has 95%+ active income
    • Operating subsidiaries with genuine substance work well; avoid IP-heavy or passive-income CFCs
  • US parent → structure for GILTI High-Tax Exclusion (≥18.9% ETR)
    • Push CFC effective rates above 18.9% to exclude from GILTI; consider FDII for US-based exports
  • Mexico parent → ensure foreign subs have >75% active income and pay >22.5% local rate
  • Brazil parent → no holding structure avoids universal CFC; focus on FTC maximization and JCP

Debt Structuring

Interest Deductibility & Thin Capitalization

EBITDA-Based Limits
  • United States: 30% of ATI (IRC §163(j))
    • De minimis: $29M gross receipts; disallowed interest carries forward indefinitely
  • Canada (EIFEL): 30% of tax-EBITDA (ITA §18.2) — effective June 2024
    • Excess capacity carried forward 20 years; de minimis C$250K; group ratio election available
    • Applies to interest paid to both related and arm's length lenders
  • Mexico: 30% of EBITDA + DER 3:1 hybrid; disallowed interest carried forward 10 years
  • Australia: 30% of tax-EBITDA — new rules from 1 July 2024 replacing the old safe harbour DER test
