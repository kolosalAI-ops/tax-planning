# Kolosal Tax 201 — Speaker Notes

These speaker notes accompany the **Kolosal Tax 201: International Tax Planning & Cross-Border Structuring** presentation — a 32-slide intermediate-level deck covering seven jurisdictions: the United States, Canada, Indonesia, Brazil, Mexico, Australia, and Singapore. The notes are written for a senior international tax partner presenting to CFOs, tax directors, and in-house counsel.

---

## Slide 1: Title Slide

- **Intermediate-level course** on international tax planning and cross-border structuring
- **Seven jurisdictions**: US, Canada, Indonesia, Brazil, Mexico, Australia, Singapore
- Covers rate comparisons, treaty structures, repatriation mechanics, and structuring decisions
- Effective rate outcomes range from **17% to 34%** depending on structure choices
- Assumes familiarity with basic corporate tax concepts

---

## Slide 2: Why Cross-Border Tax Architecture Matters

- Global corporate tax revenue exceeds **$1.4 trillion** annually; **3,000+ bilateral treaties** in force worldwide
- Effective tax rate spreads across the seven jurisdictions: **7 to 25 percentage points** depending on income type and structure
- **OECD Pillar 2**: **15% global minimum effective tax rate** for large multinationals
- A **2-percentage-point** difference on **$100M** annual profit = **$2M/year** in perpetuity
- Structural mistakes at inception trigger exit taxes, withholding taxes, and transfer pricing adjustments simultaneously

---

## Slide 3: Corporate Tax Rates & SME Regimes

- **US**: **21%** (TCJA 2017) + state income taxes
- **Canada**: **~26.5%** combined; Small Business Deduction reduces to **12.2%** on first **$500K**
- **Indonesia**: **22%**; **19%** for qualifying publicly listed companies
- **Brazil**: **34%** combined (IRPJ **25%** + CSLL **9%**) — highest in group
- **Mexico**: **30%** flat
- **Australia**: **30%** large entities; **25%** for base-rate entities (turnover < **$50M**)
- **Singapore**: **17%** headline; partial exemption on first **SGD 300K** of chargeable income
- **Key spread**: Singapore (**17%**) to Brazil (**34%**) = **17 percentage points**

---

## Slide 4: VAT, GST & Sales Tax

- **Brazil**: Cascading PIS/COFINS up to **9.25%**, plus state ICMS and municipal ISS — varies by state/product
- **Indonesia**: PPN at **11%**
- **Mexico**: IVA at **16%** with limited zero-rated categories
- **Australia**: GST at **10%**; exemptions for financial services and fresh food
- **Singapore**: GST at **9%** (recently increased)
- **Canada**: **5% federal GST** (Alberta) up to **15% HST** (Ontario, Atlantic provinces)
- **US**: No federal VAT; state/local sales tax **0% to 10%+**, retail stage only
- **Critical planning issue**: Input credit recovery — avoid irrecoverable VAT/GST embedded in supply chain

---

## Slide 5: Payroll & Employer Social Contributions

- **Brazil**: Employer burden up to **~70%** of base salary (INSS, FGTS, 13th salary, vacation bonus, system-S levies)
- **Mexico**: IMSS contributions up to **~26.5%** of payroll
- **US**: FICA **7.65%** (Social Security + Medicare) plus federal/state unemployment taxes
- **Canada**: CPP2 + EI brings employer cost to **~7.6%**
- **Indonesia**: BPJS total employer contribution **~10.24%** (health, injury, old-age, pension, death)
- **Australia**: Superannuation guarantee at **11.5%** with legislated annual increases
- **Singapore**: CPF employer contribution up to **17%** (capped; phases down for older workers)
- These costs are often the **largest line item** after rent for shared-services centres and regional HQs

---

## Slide 6: Territorial vs. Worldwide Tax Systems

- **Worldwide systems** (US, Brazil, Mexico, Indonesia, Canada): Tax residents on global income; repatriation triggers domestic tax liability offset by foreign tax credits
- **Territorial systems**: Exempt active foreign-sourced business income from domestic tax
- **Singapore**: Clearest territorial example — foreign-sourced income exempt when remitted if taxed at source and conditions met
- **Australia**: Modified territorial — active-income exemption for non-portfolio dividends and foreign branch profits
- **Impact**: Drives holding company selection, profit repatriation timing, and cash management strategy

---

## Slide 7: CFC Rules & Why They Matter

- **All seven jurisdictions** have CFC regimes — cannot simply park profits in low-tax subsidiaries
- **US** (most complex): **Subpart F** captures passive income; **GILTI** imposes minimum tax on active income exceeding deemed return on tangible assets
- **Australia**: CFC rules target tainted income in low-tax jurisdictions
- **Canada**: **FAPI** rules tax passive income through foreign affiliates
- **Brazil**: Uniquely aggressive — taxes parent on **all** foreign subsidiary income (passive and active, regardless of foreign tax rate)
- **Indonesia/Mexico**: CFC provisions targeting specific passive-income categories
- **Singapore**: Rules focused on preventing round-tripping arrangements

---

## Slide 8: CFC Thresholds & Planning Points

- **Ownership triggers**: Typically **>50%** control by votes or value
  - **US**: >50% for CFC status; **10%+ shareholders** subject to Subpart F/GILTI
  - **Canada FAPI**: >50% control threshold
  - **Indonesia**: **25%** participation threshold in certain cases
- **US Subpart F categories**: Dividends, interest, rents, royalties, certain gains (FPHCI)
- **Planning points**:
  - Structure ownership below CFC thresholds (must be genuine, not artificial)
  - Ensure **real substance** — employees, premises, local decision-making
  - Maximize **active business exemptions** through operational substance
  - Review CFC rules in **both** parent and intermediate holding jurisdictions

---

## Slide 9: How to Pick a Holding Company Jurisdiction

- **Participation exemption**: Receive dividends/capital gains from subsidiaries tax-free — Singapore offers this via **sections 13Z and 13W**
- **Treaty network breadth**: Singapore has **90+ comprehensive treaties**; Netherlands and Luxembourg have broader networks
- **Outbound withholding taxes**: Singapore imposes **zero withholding on dividends** regardless of treaty status
- **CFC exposure**: Does the holding jurisdiction create CFC issues for the parent?
- **Exit tax / CGT treatment**: Singapore has **no capital gains tax**
- **Post-Pillar 2**: Evaluate whether holding jurisdiction's effective rate will be topped up to **15%**, eroding the benefit

---

## Slide 10: Branch vs. Subsidiary

- **Branch advantages**: Losses flow through to head office; no repatriation friction; simpler setup
- **Branch disadvantages**: PE exposure; no limited liability; generally cannot access local tax incentives (e.g., Indonesia/Singapore holidays require local incorporation)
- **Subsidiary advantages**: Limited liability; local incentive access; treaty access in own right
- **Subsidiary disadvantages**: Early losses trapped locally; profit repatriation triggers withholding taxes
- **Strategy**: Consider **branch-to-subsidiary conversion** — enter as branch to utilise losses, incorporate once profitable
- **Rule of thumb**: Subsidiary for permanent operations; branch for temporary projects or expected multi-year losses

---

## Slide 11: Withholding Taxes & Treaty Networks

- **Default domestic rates** (non-treaty): Indonesia **20%** on dividends; Brazil **15%** on interest; Australia **30%** on unfranked dividends
- **Treaty-reduced rates** (examples):
  - Singapore-Indonesia: dividends reduced to **10%**
  - US-Canada: dividends reduced to **5%** (10%+ voting stock ownership)
  - Australia-Singapore: effectively **zero** on fully franked dividends
- **Always verify**: Treaty in force and ratified; protocol amendments; **Limitation on Benefits** or **Principal Purpose Test** provisions
- **Treaty shopping** increasingly challenged by domestic anti-avoidance rules and the **OECD Multilateral Instrument**

---

## Slide 12: Repatriating $10M to a Singapore Parent

- **US**: **0%** withholding (treaty exemption, 80%+ voting stock ownership)
- **Indonesia**: **10%** treaty rate = **$1M** withheld
- **Brazil**: **0%** — dividends from taxed profits exempt from withholding
- **Australia**: **0%** if fully franked; **15%** treaty rate if unfranked
- **Canada**: **15%** general treaty rate; **5%** if Singapore parent holds 10%+ voting shares
- **Mexico**: **0%** on dividends from previously taxed earnings (new protocol)
- **Singapore receiving end**: Qualifies for **foreign-sourced income exemption** if source country headline rate is **15%+** and income was taxed
- **Total repatriation cost**: **$0** (US, Brazil) to **$1.5M** (Canada at 15%)

---

## Slide 13: Foreign Tax Credit Mechanics

- **Credit method** (US, IRC sections 901-908): Residence country taxes global income, allows credit for foreign taxes paid up to domestic tax on that income
- **Exemption method** (Singapore): Excludes qualifying foreign income from tax base via **Foreign-Sourced Income Exemption**
- **Hybrid** (Australia): Credits for included foreign income; exempts non-portfolio dividends and foreign branch profits
- **Canada**: Choice between credit and deduction — credit almost always more valuable; deduction useful when excess credits would be wasted
- **Key issue**: **Excess credits** when foreign rate exceeds domestic rate — match high-tax and low-tax income streams; time income recognition to maximize utilisation

---

## Slide 14: Interest Deductibility & Thin Capitalization

- **EBITDA-based limits** (OECD BEPS Action 4 standard):
  - **US**: Net interest capped at **30% of adjusted taxable income** (section 163(j))
  - **Australia**: Moving to **30% of EBITDA**
  - **Brazil**: **30% of EBITDA** limit
  - **Canada**: EIFEL rules at **30% EBITDA** threshold
- **Debt-to-equity approach**: **Indonesia** — **4:1** ratio (debt cannot exceed 4x equity)
- **Transfer pricing**: Intercompany interest rates must be **arm's length** — document terms, security, pricing benchmarks
- **Hybrid instrument risk**: Instruments treated as debt in one jurisdiction and equity in another create **deduction-without-inclusion mismatches**

---

## Slide 15: Where to Place Your R&D Hub

- **Australia**: **43.5%** refundable offset (turnover < **$20M**); **38.5%** non-refundable for larger entities — **18.5% premium** above standard rate for eligible SMEs
- **US**: **20%** Qualified Research Tax Credit on incremental expenses; **14%** Alternative Simplified Credit
- **Canada SR&ED**: **35%** ITC for CCPCs on first **$3M** qualifying expenditures; **15%** thereafter
- **Singapore**: Enhanced deductions up to **250%** on qualifying R&D expenditure (Enterprise Innovation Scheme)
- **Key considerations**: Definition of qualifying expenditure; IP ownership implications; cost-plus transfer pricing to push R&D costs to best-incentive jurisdiction while retaining IP in favourable location

---

## Slide 16: Special Incentives & Concessionary Rates

- **Indonesia Tax Holiday**: **100% CIT exemption** for **5-20 years** for pioneer industries above capital investment thresholds
- **Singapore Pioneer Certificate**: Concessionary rates of **5% or 10%** (vs. standard 17%) for EDB-approved manufacturing, services, or HQ activities
- **Mexico IMMEX/Maquiladora**: Favourable transfer pricing safe harbours for foreign manufacturers
- **Brazil**:
  - **Manaus Free Trade Zone**: IRPJ and IPI reductions for Amazon manufacturing
  - **SUDENE/SUDAM**: Income tax reductions up to **75%** for northeast/north investments
- **Australia**: Early Stage Venture Capital Limited Partnership concessionary treatment
- **All incentives require genuine economic substance** — real operations, employees, and investment

---

## Slide 17: Global Minimum Tax — What Changes in 2025

- **OECD Pillar 2 / GloBE rules**: **15% minimum effective tax rate** for MNE groups with consolidated revenue **>EUR 750M**
- **Three interlocking rules**: Income Inclusion Rule (IIR), Undertaxed Profits Rule (UTPR), Qualified Domestic Minimum Top-up Tax (QDMTT)
- **Implementation status**:
  - **Enacted/implementing**: Singapore, Australia, Canada
  - **Monitoring**: Indonesia, Brazil, Mexico
  - **US**: Not adopted GloBE; Corporate Alternative Minimum Tax (**15%** on adjusted financial statement income) under Inflation Reduction Act serves a similar function
- **Consequence**: Every incentive, holiday, and concessionary rate must be re-evaluated against the **15% jurisdiction-level floor**

---

## Slide 18: Transfer Pricing Documentation Requirements

- **OECD three-tiered standard**: **Master File** (global overview), **Local File** (transactional analysis), **Country-by-Country Reporting** (revenue, profit, tax, employees by jurisdiction)
- **All seven jurisdictions** require some form of TP documentation
- **Australia**: Most aggressive enforcer — **Diverted Profits Tax** at **40% penalty rate** on artificially diverted profits
- **Brazil**: Aligned with OECD arm's-length principles effective **January 2024** (Law 14,596/2023), replacing prior fixed-margin system
- **Singapore**: Simplified requirements for revenue < **SGD 10M** and related-party transactions < **SGD 15M**
- **US**: Contemporaneous documentation required to avoid penalties under **section 6662(e)**
- **Penalties**: Range from modest fixed amounts to **50-100%** of underpaid tax in some jurisdictions

---

## Slide 19: Permanent Establishment Risk

- **Construction PE thresholds**: **6 months** (Indonesia, Mexico); **12 months** (Australia, Canada)
- **Agent PE**: Person habitually exercising authority to conclude contracts on behalf of foreign enterprise
- **Service PE**: Employees present providing services beyond **183 days** (typical threshold)
- **Digital PE**: Indonesia taxes certain digital activities via electronic transaction tax; Australia expanding significant economic presence concept
- **Post-COVID risk**: Remote workers in foreign jurisdictions can inadvertently create PE
- **Mitigation**: Limit employee contract-conclusion authority; use commissionaire arrangements; monitor travel days and service durations; **review PE exposure annually**

---

## Slide 20: Foreign Investment Screening & Restrictions

- **US (CFIUS)**: Reviews transactions resulting in foreign control; mandatory filing for certain sectors
- **Canada (Investment Canada Act)**: Reviews significant investments; net-benefit threshold adjusted annually
- **Indonesia**: Priority Investment List specifies open/restricted/closed sectors with max foreign ownership percentages
- **Australia (FIRB)**: Screens above threshold amounts; special rules for agricultural land, residential real estate, sensitive sectors
- **Mexico**: Restricts foreign participation in energy, telecom, and strategic sectors
- **Land ownership restrictions**: Indonesia and Mexico prohibit direct foreign land ownership — alternative structures required
- **Approval timelines**: **30 days to 12 months** depending on jurisdiction and complexity

---

## Slide 21: Non-Resident Capital Gains Tax on Share Disposal

- **Singapore**: **No capital gains tax** — gains on share disposals not taxable (any holding period or percentage)
- **Australia**: Tax on shares where underlying value principally attributable to Australian real property (section 855-10)
- **US (FIRPTA)**: Tax on disposal of US Real Property Holding Corporation interests; **15% withholding**
- **Indonesia**: **5%** on gross proceeds (listed shares); **25%** on net gains (unlisted)
- **Canada**: Tax on Taxable Canadian Property (shares deriving **>50%** value from Canadian real property)
- **Mexico**: **25%** on gross proceeds or **35%** on net gain (non-resident's choice)
- **Brazil**: Listed shares on exchange generally exempt; OTC/unlisted taxed at **15-22.5%**

---

## Slide 22: Net Operating Loss Rules & Strategy

- **US**: **Indefinite** carryforward; limited to **80%** of taxable income per year (TCJA); carryback eliminated (except farming)
- **Canada**: **20-year** carryforward; **3-year** carryback
- **Indonesia**: **5-year** carryforward (standard); **10 years** in special economic zones
- **Brazil**: **Unlimited** carryforward; capped at **30%** of annual taxable income (~4 years minimum to fully utilise)
- **Mexico**: **10-year** carryforward with **inflation adjustment**
- **Australia**: **Indefinite** carryforward; subject to **continuity of ownership** and same-business tests (ownership changes >50% can forfeit losses)
- **Singapore**: **Indefinite** carryforward; subject to **substantial shareholder continuity** test
- **Model the loss recovery timeline** under local rules — directly affects project IRR

---

## Slide 23: Accelerated Depreciation & Capital Allowances

- **US**: Section 179 immediate expensing up to **$1.29M** (2024); bonus depreciation **60%** first-year (2024), declining **20 pp/year** until 2027 phase-out
- **Australia**: Instant asset write-off for assets < **$20K** (small businesses); temporary enhancements have varied
- **Indonesia**: Accelerated depreciation in SEZs using **double-declining-balance**, cutting standard useful lives in half
- **Canada**: Accelerated Investment Incentive + immediate expensing up to **$1.5M** for CCPCs
- **Singapore**: Section 19A accelerated write-off over **1 or 3 years** for qualifying plant and machinery
- **Mexico/Brazil**: Standard declining-balance depreciation without aggressive acceleration
- **Impact**: For capital-intensive operations, depreciation regimes can shift **tens of millions** in tax liability between periods

---

## Slide 24: Business Expenses & Deduction Rules

- **Meals & entertainment**: US allows **50%** deduction for business meals, **zero** for entertainment (TCJA); Canada similarly **50%** limit
- **Charitable contributions**:
  - **US**: Capped at **10%** of taxable income
  - **Canada**: Up to **75%** of net income
  - **Australia**: **Unlimited** for gifts to registered Deductible Gift Recipients
  - **Brazil**: Deductions only for government-approved cultural/social programmes
- Most other jurisdictions allow reasonable business entertainment as deductible
- **Indonesia/Brazil**: Category-specific limitations on certain expenses
- **Action item**: Review global travel and expense policies against each jurisdiction's tax code to maximize deductions and avoid disallowed categories

---

## Slide 25: Taxation by Income Source Type

- **Active business income**: Generally taxed at standard CIT rate in the jurisdiction where activity occurs
- **Passive income** (dividends, interest, royalties, rents, capital gains): Different treatment across jurisdictions
  - **US CFC**: Passive income triggers immediate **Subpart F** inclusion, bypassing deferral
  - **Canada FAPI**: Taxes Canadian shareholders on passive income through foreign affiliates
- **Dividend participation exemptions**: Singapore exempts qualifying foreign dividends; Australia exempts non-portfolio dividends via active-income test
- **Interest/royalty income**: Subject to **withholding tax** in paying jurisdiction — evaluate corridor by corridor
- **Treaty access**: Different treaty articles apply to business profits, dividends, interest, and royalties — each with different rates and conditions

---

## Slide 26: Sector-Specific Taxes & Levies

- **Natural resources**:
  - **Indonesia**: Coal royalties **3-7%** of sales revenue (by type/calorific value) on top of **22% CIT**
  - **Australia**: Petroleum Resource Rent Tax at **40%** on offshore oil and gas profits
  - **Mexico**: Mining duties at **7.5% of EBITDA** + **0.5%** on gold/silver revenue
  - **Brazil**: ANP royalties on oil and gas production
- **Financial services**: US **BEAT** provision disproportionately affects banks with significant cross-border payments
- **Digital services**: Indonesia — digital VAT on foreign providers; Australia — GST on low-value digital imports; Mexico — specific digital PE concepts
- **Go beyond headline CIT rate** — model sector-specific levies, royalties, and compliance obligations

---

## Slide 27: International Investor Scorecard

- **Scored across 6 dimensions**: CIT competitiveness, treaty network breadth, incentive attractiveness, administrative ease, capital gains treatment, Pillar 2 readiness
- **Singapore**: Leads overall — low rate, extensive treaties, no CGT, territorial system
- **Australia**: Strong franking credits and R&D incentives; less competitive on CFC rules and **30%** headline rate
- **US**: Unmatched market access + FDII benefit; high complexity cost
- **Canada**: Strong SR&ED incentives; reasonable SME rates
- **Indonesia**: Aggressive tax holidays; administrative burden and legal uncertainty remain concerns
- **Mexico**: Genuine maquiladora advantages for manufacturing; limited appeal for other activities
- **Brazil**: Lowest score on administrative burden; underappreciated **0%** dividend withholding and unlimited loss carryforward

## Slide 28: Choosing Your Structure — 4 Key Questions

1. **Where is your ultimate parent?** — Determines worldwide vs. territorial system and applicable CFC rules (US parent = GILTI/Subpart F; Singapore parent = territorial exemptions)
2. **What activity will you conduct?** — Manufacturing, services, R&D, holding, and financing each qualify for different incentives and face different PE risks
3. **What is your profitability timeline?** — Multi-year losses favour branch structure (loss flow-through); check local carryforward period (Indonesia's **5 years** may be insufficient)
4. **What is your exit strategy?** — Structure through **no-CGT jurisdiction** (Singapore) for eventual sale; consider listing jurisdiction for IPO scenarios

## Slide 29: GAAR, Anti-Treaty Shopping & Substance

- **Australia Part IVA**: Applies to any scheme where dominant purpose was obtaining a tax benefit
- **Canada GAAR**: Targets transactions complying with the letter but abusing the spirit of the law
- **US section 7701(o)**: Codified economic substance doctrine — transaction must change economic position meaningfully and have **substantial non-tax business purpose**
- **Indonesia**: Specific anti-avoidance rules allowing DGT to recharacterise transactions
- **Principal Purpose Test** (OECD MLI): Applies to most treaties — benefit denied if one of the principal purposes was obtaining that treaty benefit
- **Common thread**: **Substance** — real operations, real employees, real decisions, genuine economic rationale. Document commercial purpose contemporaneously.

## Slide 30: Cross-Border Tax Planning Checklist

**Pre-Entry:**
- Select entity type (branch vs. subsidiary analysis)
- Map treaty corridors for optimal withholding rates
- Establish transfer pricing policy with arm's-length benchmarks before first intercompany transaction
- Complete regulatory screenings and foreign investment approvals
- Document substance requirements (decision-making location, employees, physical presence)

**Operational Phase:**
- Maintain contemporaneous TP documentation (Master File, Local File, CbCR)
- Monitor PE exposure (employee travel days, agent activities)
- Establish compliance calendar (filings, estimated payments, incentive renewals)
- Track and allocate foreign tax credits to correct baskets and periods
- Review structure periodically against legislative changes

**Repatriation & Exit:**
- Optimise withholding taxes on dividends, interest, and royalties via treaty networks
- Plan capital gains tax exposure before initiating any disposal
- Compare liquidation vs. share sale vs. asset sale for tax efficiency
- Document IP transfers at arm's-length values
- Assess Pillar 2 impact on overall effective tax rate
