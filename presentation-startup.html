Contract Design

SaaS Contract Structuring to Minimize WHT

Safe Contract Language
• “Access to hosted application” not “license of software”
• “Subscription to cloud service” not “right to use software”
• No source code access clause — prevents royalty recharacterization
• Data stays on provider’s servers — no local installation
• Service-level agreements (SLAs) emphasize availability, not IP access
• Implementation fees billed separately as “professional services”
Dangerous Contract Language
• ✗ “License to use the software” → royalty WHT in ID/BR
• ✗ “Right to copy, modify, or distribute” → royalty under most treaties
• ✗ “Perpetual license” or “on-premise deployment option”
• ✗ “Access to underlying algorithms/source code”
• ✗ Mixed contracts bundling SaaS + on-premise without allocation
• ✗ “Technology transfer” or “know-how” language

IP Migration

IP Migration: Cost-Sharing vs Buy-In

Cost-Sharing Arrangement (CSA)
  • Mechanism: Participants share R&D costs proportional to anticipated benefits (by revenue or profit split)
  • Advantage: No upfront lump-sum payment — ongoing cost allocation. IRS §482 Reg. 1.482-7 governs
  • Risk: Buy-in payment required for pre-existing IP at arm’s length value. Platform Contribution Transactions (PCTs)
  • Best for: Post-revenue startups with significant existing IP needing gradual migration
Outright Buy-In
  • Mechanism: Lump-sum purchase of IP rights at fair market value, typically via independent valuation (DCF)
  • Advantage: Clean transfer, clear IP ownership. Buyer gets amortization deduction on purchase price
  • Risk: High valuation = large taxable gain for seller. Exit charges in some jurisdictions (AU Div 855)
  • Best for: Pre-revenue startups (IP value ~ $0), or restructuring into principal model at growth stage
Golden rule: Assign IP to your principal entity on Day 0 at incorporation. Pre-revenue, pre-product IP has negligible FMV. Post-Series A, the same IP may be worth $5–50M — making migration prohibitively expensive.

Key Drivers
• US revenue is most expensive — LRD profit taxed at 21%. More US = higher group ETR

Documentation

R&D Documentation: What Auditors Actually Look For

Must Have (Day 1)
• Project descriptions: What technological uncertainty exists? What are you trying to solve?
• Time tracking: Engineer hours by project, by qualifying activity category
• Git commit history: Contemporaneous evidence of experimentation & iteration
• Design documents: Architecture decisions, alternatives evaluated, experiments run
• Failure logs: Document what didn’t work and why — this proves experimentation
Common Rejection Reasons
• ✗ “We built a new feature” — no uncertainty described
• ✗ Retroactive documentation (reconstructed 2 years later)
• ✗ Including routine development, bug fixes, UI polish
• ✗ No time allocation — claiming 100% of all eng time
• ✗ Missing nexus between spend and qualifying project
Jurisdiction-Specific Tips
• AU: Register with AusIndustry before year-end. 10-month deadline post-FY
• CA: T661 form is notoriously complex. Budget $5–15K for SR&ED consultant
• US: 4-part test documentation per project. Keep contemporaneously
• SG: EIS claims via Form C-S. Simpler but must show “new knowledge”
• ID: BRIN coordination required. 6–12 month approval process
ROI of good documentation: A $15K annual investment in R&D documentation (consultant + internal process) protects $500K–$1.3M in annual credits. Audit-proofing ratio: 30–90x return. Start with a simple Google Sheet tracking engineer time by project category — upgrade to dedicated tools (Boast.ai, Ardius, neo.tax) as you scale.

Tax Treaties
  • SUTE (Startup Tax Exemption): 75% exemption on first S$100K + 50% on next S$100K = effective 4.25% on S$200K for first 3 years
  • PTE scheme: 75% on first S$10K + 50% on next S$190K = effective 8.29% on S$200K (Years 4+)
  • EIS 400%: S$400K deduction per S$100K of qualifying R&D expenditure (until YA 2028). Covers wages, consumables, IP registration
  • Pioneer Certificate (EDB): 0–5% CIT for qualifying technology companies. Requires application and substance commitment
  • GTP (Global Trader Programme): 5–10% on qualifying trading income for companies with trading hub in SG
  • 0% CGT: No capital gains tax on disposal of shares or IP. Critical for exit planning
  • 0% dividend WHT: One-tier system — corporate tax is final, no additional tax on distribution
  • No CFC rules: Foreign subsidiary profits are not taxed in Singapore unless remitted (and exemptions apply under §13(8))
The SG stack is unmatched: SUTE (4.25%) + EIS (400% R&D) + 0% CGT + 0% div WHT + no CFC + 90+ treaties = lowest friction path from startup to exit. No other jurisdiction offers this combination for B2B SaaS.

United States

US: QSBS §1202 + R&D Credit

QSBS (Qualified Small Business Stock)
• 100% exclusion on gain from sale of QSBS (up to $10M or 10× adjusted basis per shareholder)
• Requirements: C-corporation, <$50M gross assets at issuance, held ≥5 years, business (not finance/consulting/mining)
• SaaS/software qualifies as technology business
• Founder with $100K basis → up to $10M tax-free on exit
• Stacking: each shareholder gets separate $10M/$100M exclusion
• Cannot be LLC/S-Corp — must be C-Corp from day of issuance
R&D Credit (§41)
• 20% credit on qualifying R&D expenses above base amount
• Startup payroll offset: <$5M gross receipts + <5 years → offset up to $500K/year payroll taxes (§41(h))
• Critical for pre-revenue startups with no income tax liability
• §174 trap: Since 2022, must amortize R&D over 5yr (domestic) / 15yr (foreign). Creates phantom taxable income
• Credit carry-forward: 20 years
US-founded startup strategy: Delaware C-Corp (QSBS eligibility) + maximize §41 payroll offset + push development R&D to non-US entities (avoid §174 amortization on foreign spend) + SG subsidiary for APAC revenue.

Brazil & Mexico: Startup-Specific Tax Plays

Brazil
Lucro Presumido regime: For startups with <R$78M revenue. Presume 32% of service revenue as taxable base → effective CIT ~10.88% (vs 34% Lucro Real). SaaS likely qualifies as service.

Simples Nacional: Revenue <R$4.8M. Annex III (services): starts at 6% all-in. Annex V (technology): 15.5–30.5%. Factor “r” (payroll/revenue) determines annex — high payroll ratio qualifies for lower Annex III rates.

JCP (Juros sobre Capital Próprio): Deductible notional interest on equity. TJLP rate × equity base. Reduces effective CIT to ~24–28%. Requires shareholders — works for VC-backed startups.

Lei do Bem R&D: 60–100% additional deduction on R&D spend. Must be Lucro Real. $3M R&D → $408K benefit. Auto-apply — no pre-approval needed.

Best startup role: LATAM sales hub. Lucro Presumido for service revenue. Keep IP outside Brazil (WHT on outbound royalties 15% + CIDE 10% = 25% total — expensive).
Mexico
RESICO: Simplified regime for individuals with <MXN 3.5M revenue. Rates 1–2.5%. For founder sole proprietors only, not companies.

Border zone incentive: 20% ISR rate (vs 30%) + 8% IVA (vs 16%) for businesses in northern/southern border municipalities. If your MX sales office is in Tijuana, Monterrey border: 10pp CIT savings.

R&D credit (LISR Art 202): 30% tax credit on incremental R&D expenses above base period average. Annual cap MXN 50M. Credit against ISR, non-refundable, 10-year carry-forward.

PTU (Profit-Sharing): Mandatory 10% of pre-tax profits distributed to employees. Cap at 3 months salary or avg of last 3 years. Significant cost for profitable startups — plan around it.

Best startup role: LATAM sales sub (LRD). TNMM 5% operating margin limits PTU exposure. Nearshore dev center (engineering talent at $40–80K/yr). Use border zone if possible.
LATAM = sales only: Keep IP out of BR and MX. High outbound royalty WHT (BR 25%, MX 25–35%) makes IP ownership expensive. Use LRD model at 5% OM to limit local tax exposure while capturing LATAM revenue through the SG principal.

Singapore

SG HoldCo: Substance Requirements

  • Minimum requirements: At least 1 resident director (Singapore citizen/PR/EP holder), registered office address, company secretary, annual filing with ACRA. Virtual office acceptable initially but physical presence strengthens substance
  • Economic substance (post-BEPS): IRAS expects key decision-making in Singapore — board meetings, strategic IP decisions, pricing authority. Employ qualified individuals who manage the IP and business operations. Not just a mailbox
  • For IP ownership: Demonstrate DEMPE functions (Development, Enhancement, Maintenance, Protection, Exploitation) are performed or controlled from Singapore. At minimum: CTO or VP Eng based in SG, IP strategy decisions documented in SG board minutes
  • Cost & timeline: Incorporation: S$300–500 + 1–2 days. Corporate secretary: S$300–600/yr. Virtual office: S$200–500/mo. Bank account: 2–4 weeks (DBS/OCBC). Annual filing: S$1–3K. Total Year 1 setup: S$5–15K
  • EDB engagement: For Pioneer Certificate (0–5% CIT), engage with Economic Development Board. Requires commitment to hire in SG, invest in local R&D. Approval typically 3–6 months
Substance is the foundation: SG’s benefits only hold if IRAS sees real economic substance. A mailbox company will be challenged. Minimum viable substance: 1 resident director (EP holder), quarterly board meetings in SG, 2+ employees making IP/pricing decisions. Cost: S$10–15K/year.

Pre-Incorporation

Pre-Incorporation Tax Checklist

  • Founder residency analysis — determine home country CFC exposure, personal tax residency, social security obligations
  • Entity jurisdiction selection — SG HoldCo vs US Delaware vs Hybrid based on revenue target, VC source, exit plan
  • IP assignment on Day 0 — assign all pre-existing IP to the principal entity at incorporation. Document FMV = $0 (pre-revenue, pre-product)
  • ESOP pool design — 10–20% option pool. Choose ISO vs NSO (US), determine vesting schedule, get initial 409A valuation if US entity
  • VC term sheet tax review — preferred share structure, anti-dilution, liquidation preference. Check SAFE/convertible cross-border treatment
  • Initial TP policy draft — even pre-revenue, document the intended principal model. Sets arm’s length framework from Day 1
  • Tax treaty map — identify treaty rates for planned sales jurisdictions (dividend WHT, royalty WHT, service PE thresholds)
  • Bank account setup — SG: DBS/OCBC (2–4 weeks). US: Mercury/SVB (1 week). Open in principal jurisdiction first
Day 0 is everything: IP assignment at $0 valuation, ESOP pool creation, and TP policy draft cost virtually nothing pre-revenue. Post-Series A, these same actions can cost millions in transfer taxes and restructuring fees.

Year 1–2 Operational Tax Checklist

  • R&D credit documentation from Day 1 — track qualifying activities contemporaneously: project descriptions, time allocation, technological uncertainty

  • Economic substance evidence — SG board minutes, key decisions documented in SG, employees hired. Avoid “brass plate” perception

Growth Stage Tax Checklist

  • Principal structure activation — establish LRD entities in key sales markets. Sign intercompany agreements. Set TNMM 5% OM benchmarks
  • IP migration if needed — execute CSA or buy-in. Get independent valuation. Earlier = cheaper
  • APA consideration — for flows >$10M, Advance Pricing Agreements with IRAS, IRS, ATO
  • Pillar 2 readiness — track revenue vs €750M threshold. Build SBIE carve-out (payroll in low-tax entities)
  • Multi-jurisdiction ESOP — local plan sub-documents per country. Securities filings (Form D US, AU ASIC, SG s273)
  • Exit structure planning — SG listing (0% CGT), US IPO (FIRPTA), dual listing. Track QSBS 5-year hold periods

  • TP benchmarking study — formal benchmarking for all LRDs. Update annually. Budget $15–30K
Growth stage = highest risk: Intercompany flows become material, tax authorities pay attention, and restructuring gets expensive. Lock in APAs for major corridors — the $50–100K cost prevents $1M+ TP adjustments.
Pitfalls

Common Startup Tax Mistakes

  • IP assignment too late: Founders build product for 2 years, raise Series A, then try to assign IP to SG HoldCo. IP now worth $10M+ → capital gains tax on transfer. $2–5M cost
  • Ignoring TP from Day 1: No intercompany agreements, no documentation. Tax authority audit at Series C → TP adjustments + penalties (up to 200% in Indonesia, 40% in US). Audit risk
  • Choosing entity based on VC preference alone: US VC says “we only invest in Delaware C-Corps” → founder ignores 21% CIT + §174 amortization + GILTI. Should evaluate hybrid structures first. Avoidable
  • Not claiming R&D credits: AU startup leaving $870K/year cash on the table. CA startup not filing SR&ED. US startup not using §41 payroll offset. Cash left on table
  • Single-entity international sales: SG company with US sales reps = dependent agent PE in US. Should have established US LRD before first hire. PE exposure
  • ESOP without local compliance: US ISO plan applied globally. Employees in AU/SG/CA face unexpected tax at exercise because plan doesn’t meet local favorable treatment requirements. Employee impact
Prevention cost vs cure cost: Proper Day 0 structuring costs $10–30K. IP migration at Series B costs $500K–5M. TP audit adjustments at growth stage cost $1M+. The ROI on early tax planning is 50–100x.

Optimal Repatriation Stack
Priority order (lowest cost first):
1. Intercompany service fees — 0% WHT in most corridors. Deductible by payer
2. Royalties — 0% under SG-US/CA treaties. Deductible by payer
3. Loan repayment — return of capital, no WHT on principal
4. Dividends — last resort (non-deductible by payer, WHT applies)
ID & BR: Special Considerations
Indonesia: Bank Indonesia reporting for FX >$25K. Commercial bank handles. No approval needed for current account transactions. Plan: Quarterly service fee invoicing to SG.

Brazil: BCB RDE-IED registration required for all foreign investment. IOF tax on some FX transactions (0.38% general). Plan: 0% dividend WHT is BR’s unique advantage — use it.
Repatriation priority: Service fees first (0% WHT, deductible), then royalties (0% for SG-US/CA), then loan repayment (principal = no WHT), dividends last (non-deductible, WHT applies). This ordering alone saves 5–15% on intercompany cash flows.
