- **Mexico** applies 10% on dividends from post-2013 CUFIN profits (**LISR Art. 164**).

## 6. Net Operating Loss (NOL) Rules

| Parameter | US | Canada | Indonesia | Brazil | Mexico | Australia | Singapore |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Carry-Forward** | Indefinite | 20 years | **5 years** | Indefinite | 10 years | Indefinite | Indefinite |
| **Carry-Back** | None (post-TCJA) | 3 years | None | None | None | 1 year | 1 year (S$100K cap) |
| **Annual Limit** | 80% of taxable income | 100% | 100% | **30%** of taxable income | 100% | 100% | 100% |
| **Continuity Test** | None | N/A (no specific test) | Change >50% ownership forfeits losses | Losses non-transferable in M&A | Corporate restructuring restrictions | COT or BCT/SBT | Shareholder test |

### 6.1 Statutory References for NOL

| Country | Carry-Forward | Carry-Back | Annual Limit | Citation |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **US** | Indefinite | None | 80% | **IRC §172(a)/(b)** (NOL deduction, 80% limit post-TCJA) |
| **Canada** | 20 years | 3 years | 100% | **ITA §111(1)(a)** (non-capital losses); **ITA §111(1)(b)** (net capital losses, indefinite) |
| **Indonesia** | 5 years | None | 100% | **UU 36/2008 Pasal 6(2)** (5-year carry-forward) |
| **Brazil** | Indefinite | None | 30% | **RIR/2018 Art. 320** (indefinite CF); **Art. 321** (30% limit) |
| **Mexico** | 10 years | None | 100% | **LISR Art. 57** (10-year carry-forward) |
| **Australia** | Indefinite | 1 year | 100% | **ITAA 1997 Div. 36** (losses); **Div. 160** (carry-back); **§160-5** (turnover <$5B) |
| **Singapore** | Indefinite | 1 year (S$100K) | 100% | **ITA §37(1)** (carry-forward, indefinite); **§37(3)** (carry-back, 1 year, S$100K cap) |

### 6.2 Key Observations

- **Brazil's 30% annual cap** (RIR/2018 Art. 321) is the most restrictive -- even with indefinite carry-forward, large accumulated losses take many years to fully absorb.
- **Indonesia's 5-year expiry** (UU 36/2008 Pasal 6(2)) can result in permanent loss of deductions for companies with prolonged downturns.
- **The US 80% limit** (IRC §172(b)) means profitable US corporations always pay some tax even with prior losses.
- **Singapore, Australia, and Canada** offer the most flexible NOL regimes -- combining indefinite carry-forward with carry-back options and no annual utilization cap.

### 7.3 Key R&D Observations

- **Singapore's EIS** (400% deduction from YA 2024) is the most generous percentage deduction globally. Combined with the 17% CIT rate, the effective subsidy rate is high.
- **Canada's SR&ED** (ITA §127) is uniquely valuable for small CCPCs because the 35% credit is **refundable** -- the government pays cash even with no tax liability.
- **The US** shifted R&D costs from immediate expensing to **5-year amortization** under IRC §174 starting 2022, increasing the after-tax cost of R&D.
- **Indonesia's 300% super deduction** (PP 45/2019) is generous but requires formal approval by BRIN (National Research and Innovation Agency).

## 8. Interest Deduction Limitations

### 8.1 Thin Capitalization & EBITDA Rules

| Country | Primary Rule | Ratio/Limit | Legal Citation |
|---|---|---|---|
| **US** | EBITDA-based | **30%** of adjusted taxable income (ATI) | **IRC §163(j)(1)-(3)** (business interest limitation); ATI = EBITDA through 2025, then EBIT |
| **Canada** | EBITDA-based (EIFEL) | **30%** of tax EBITDA (from June 2023); ratio of **40%** for first transitional year | **ITA §18.2** (EIFEL rules -- Excessive Interest and Financing Expenses Limitation) |
| **Indonesia** | DER-based | **4:1** debt-to-equity ratio | **PMK 169/2015** (4:1 DER limit); interest on excess debt non-deductible |
| **Brazil** | DER-based | **2:1** (related parties); **0.3:1** (tax haven lenders) | **Lei 12.249/2010 Art. 24-26** (thin capitalization rules) |
| **Mexico** | DER + EBITDA | **3:1** DER; also **30%** EBITDA limit on net interest | **LISR Art. 28(XXVII)** (3:1 DER); **LISR Art. 28(XXXII)** (30% EBITDA from 2020) |
| **Australia** | Transitioning to EBITDA | Old: **60%** safe harbour debt; New (from July 2024): **30%** of tax EBITDA | **ITAA 1997 §820-37** (old 60% safe harbour); **§820-46** (new 30% EBITDA from 1 July 2024) |
| **Singapore** | **None** (arm's length only) | No fixed DER or EBITDA ratio; interest must be on arm's length terms | **ITA §33** (GAAR); **§34D-34F** (transfer pricing provisions) |

### 8.2 Key Observations on Interest Limitation

- **Singapore** is uniquely permissive -- it has **no thin capitalization rules** and **no EBITDA-based interest limitation**. Interest deductions are only scrutinized under arm's length transfer pricing principles (**ITA §34D-34F**).
- **The US, Canada, Mexico, and Australia** have all converged on **30% of EBITDA** rules, aligned with OECD BEPS Action 4 recommendations.
- **Indonesia and Brazil** use traditional **debt-to-equity ratio** approaches. Brazil's 2:1 (Lei 12.249/2010 Art. 24) is stricter than Indonesia's 4:1 (PMK 169/2015).
- **Mexico** uniquely combines **both** a DER test (3:1) and an EBITDA test (30%), applying whichever is more restrictive.
- **Carry-forward of denied deductions**: US (indefinite), Canada (20 years), Australia (15 years), Mexico (10 years). Singapore, Indonesia, and Brazil do not allow carry-forward of denied interest.

## 14. Key Strategic Observations

### 14.1 Lowest Effective CIT Burden

For a technology company with significant R&D spending:

1. **Singapore**: 0-5% (Pioneer Status, ITA §13(1)) or 17% minus EIS deductions, effective ~5-10%
2. **Indonesia**: 0% for 5-20 years (Tax Holiday, PMK 130/2020), then 22%
3. **Canada**: 9% federal + provincial (CCPC) with 35% refundable SR&ED credit (ITA §127(10.1))
4. **United States**: 21% flat, but R&D credit (IRC §41) and FDII (IRC §250, 13.125%) reduce effective rate
5. **Australia**: 25% (base rate entity) with 18.5% R&D offset (ITAA 1997 Div. 355)
6. **Mexico**: 30% with limited R&D benefit (LISR Art. 202)
7. **Brazil**: 34% (though Lei do Bem reduces effective burden)

> **Strategic Summary**: Singapore and Brazil stand out at opposite ends — Singapore imposes no CGT, no dividend tax, no FTT, no insurance premium tax, and no agricultural special regime, relying on its low 17% CIT as the universal rate. Brazil layers the most sector-specific taxes (IOF on credit/FX/insurance, CFEM mining, FUNRURAL agriculture, sports betting GGR) on top of its already high combined 34% CIT. Canada is unique with its 3% DST. Australia's PRRT at 40% is the highest natural resource tax. Indonesia and Mexico use final tax mechanisms to simplify compliance for specific transaction types.
